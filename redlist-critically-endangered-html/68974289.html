Taxonomy [top]

Kingdom Phylum Class Order Family
Animalia Arthropoda Insecta Hemiptera Pseudococcidae

Scientific Name: Pseudococcus markharveyi
Species Authority: Gullan, 2013
Common Name(s):
English – Banksia montana Mealybug
Taxonomic Notes: Pseudococcus markharveyi has recently been described (Gullan et al. 2013) and has been previously referred to as Pseudococcus sp. 15 in Moir et al. (2012).

Assessment Information [top]

Red List Category & Criteria: Critically Endangered B2ab(i,ii,iii,iv,v) ver 3.1
Year Published: 2015
Date Assessed: 2014-03-12
Assessor(s): Moir, M. & Leng, M.C.
Reviewer(s): Gerlach, J.
Justification:
Pseudococcus markharveyi is at risk of extinction as its likely host breadth is very low (one host plant species; Moir et al. 2011). We have used the host-breadth models of Vesk et al. (2010), which assess host breadth in light of uncertainty (such as the potential of the insect to be a tourist and not actually feeding on any given plant species that it is found on), to examine this. Coupled with the low dispersal ability, the fragmented and threatened nature of the host plant subpopulations, the probability of extinction is high.

This species is assessed as Critically Endangered based on criterion B. It has an area of occupancy (AOO) of 8 km2, calculated using a 2 km x 2 km grid. The population is severely fragmented and continuing declines are inferred in the AOO, EOO, habitat, number of subpopulations and number of mature individuals.

Geographic Range [top]

Range Description:Pseudococcus markharveyi has been recorded from two subpopulations in the Stirling Range National Park (SRNP) of the southwest biodiversity hotspot of Western Australia. At one site (Bluff Knoll), the bug has only been recorded from one individual of Banksia montana. In 2012 the mealybug was a part of a translocation trial. It has since been successfully established at Kamballup (c. 40 km to the south of SRNP), under intensive management.

The total area occupied by the species, based on the area of occupied subpopulations of the host plant, is under 1 km2. However, using the standard IUCN method of a 2 km x 2 km grid, the area of occupancy (AOO) is 8 km2. Each subpopulation represents a threat-based location.

Population [top]

Population:Numbers of individuals have not been counted as this is hard to achieve due their small size and rarity. As there are no estimates of population size it is not known if there has been any reduction in numbers.

The distribution of Pseudococcus markharveyi is severely fragmented due to its very narrow host-breath association with its host plant B. montana. To date the natural known subpopulations of P. markharveyi are found at two peaks in the SRNP (ie: Bluff Knoll and Pyungorup Peak) and these B. montana subpopulations have an area of under 1 km2. At one of these subpopulations of P. markharveyi is found solely on one plant, despite other mature plants available for colonization. To date these B. montana subpopulations (and therefore P. markharveyi subpopulations) are under threat from Phytophthora dieback disease, inappropriate fire regimes (Barrett et al. 2008), fragmentation and, due to their montane habitat, potentially climate change. A third subpopulation has been established at a trial translocation site 40 km to the south of SRNP (see Moir 2014).

Habitat and Ecology [top]

Habitat and Ecology:Pseudococcus markharveyi is closely associated with its only known host plant B. montana which is found growing in montane habitats in the SRNP. It is a component of the endangered Eastern Stirling Range Montane Heath and Thicket Threatened Ecological Community (Gilfillan et al. 2008). It can be found growing in rocky shallow brown loam on schist/quartz in association with dense montane heath/kwongan (Kershaw et al. 1997). Banksia montana is a State ‘Declared Rare’ plant due to its fragmented and restricted distribution in southwest Australia. The host plant is highly susceptible to the introduced plant pathogen, Phytophthora dieback, and is listed nationally as ‘Endangered’ under the Environmental Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999, and is also listed as ‘Critically Endangered’ under the Western Australian Wildlife Conservation Act 1953. As of 2004, four subpopulations of B. montana have been recorded, containing a total of 45 adult and 16 juvenile plants (Gilfillan et al. 2008).

All mealybugs are sap-suckers that are often closely linked with their host plants. As they are also relatively sedentary they make easy prey for a range of predators. The most frequent being nabid bugs, brown and green lacewing larvae, coccinellid beetles, and spiders.

Threats [top]

Major Threat(s): Pseudococcus markharveyi is closely associated with its only known host plant B. montana. Therefore, threats to B. montana subpopulations, such as climate change, plant diseases, fragmentation and inappropriate fire regime indirectly threaten P. markharveyi.

Climate change (current and future threat)
Montane habitats of B. montana (e.g. SRNP) are at risk of drying out, as they currently have higher stable humidity than lower altitudes (Moir and Leng 2013). This change in atmospheric conditions may cause B. montana and/or P. markharveyi individuals to die.

Phytophthora dieback disease and other plant diseases (past, current and future threat)
Banksia montana is highly susceptible to the introduced plant pathogen, Phytophthora cinnamomi. The loss of B. montana individuals will result in loss of ‘habitat’ (host plants) for P. markharveyi. The Bluff Knoll subpopulation is under the most severe threat from this disease (see Barrett and Yates on-line early), with substantial habitat alteration. Bluff Knoll, and to a lesser extent, the other peaks that contain B. montana and P. markharveyi, experience heavy tourist traffic, and results in dieback being transported across the area.

Fragmentation (past, current and future threat)
Although Gullan et al. (2013) demonstrated that genetically the two subpopulations of P. markharveyi are indistinct, the subpopulations were likely once part of a continuous thicket of montane heath. Dieback and fire have fragmented the subpopulations. The two subpopulations are too far apart for P. markharveyi to recolonise if it becomes locally extinct at one site. For example, at Bluff Knoll the mealybug has remained on the oldest B. montana since the last fire (c. 14 yrs) but has not been able to colonise the other mature B. montana present although they are less than 90 m apart.

Wildfires and inappropriate fire regimes (past, current and future threat)
All host plant subpopulations (and therefore P. markharveyi subpopulations) are at risk of wildfires. Fire will extinguish available host plant structures and the bug directly, or indirectly, through the loss of available above ground plant material. Natural recolonisation will not occur due to fragmented nature of P. markharveyi subpopulations and the sessile nature of the mealybug (see above). Fire and dieback disease interact to cause the local extinction of Banksia plants (see Barrett and Yates on-line early) further facilitating possibly local extinction of both plant and bug.

Predators
The ladybird beetles Coccinella transversalis and the introduced Coccinella undecimpunctata are a voracious predators of Pseudococcus markharveyi (M. Moir pers. obs. 2014), particularly on Bluff Knoll. However, the level of predation has not been quantified and the overall threat is unknown.

Conservation Actions [top]

Conservation Actions: Pseudococcus markharveyi has been listed by the State government (Western Australia) as a threatened species under the category of ‘Critically Endangered’ in 2013.
A translocation trial occurred in 2012 to determine whether P. markharveyi could be successfully introduced to another site. The translocation site is approximately 40 km south from the SRNP subpopulation, and at a much lower altitude. To date successful translocation of P. markharveyi has been achieved ex situ (c. 40% success rate; Moir 2014) on the B. montana individuals located here.

To date no natural areas (SRNP) are actively managed for the mealybug. Pseudococcus markharveyi should benefit from the management of subpopulations of its host plant B. montana as to date it has only been recorded from this plant host (Moir et al. 2011). To ensure P. markharveyi survival and recovery there is a need to protect and manage the host plant B. montana from threats such as Phytophthora dieback disease, inappropriate fire regimes (Barrett et al. 2008), fragmentation and due to their montane habitat, potentially climate change. Any threat to B. montana can be considered an indirect threat to P. markharveyi.

Finally, we also note that loss of P. markharveyi occurs BEFORE that of the host plant (most mature B. montana on Bluff Knoll do not have any mealybugs) and therefore there may be a need to reintroduce the bug onto individual B. montana when the population is scattered.
