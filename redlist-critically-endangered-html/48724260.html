Assessment Information [top]

Red List Category & Criteria: Critically Endangered A2ac ver 3.1
Year Published: 2013
Date Assessed: 2013-06-26
Assessor(s): Joslin, J.D., Haber, W.A. & Hamilton, D.
Reviewer(s): Pollock, C.M.
Justification:
Ocotea monteverdensis is endemic to Costa Rica where it is restricted to premontane wet forest areas between 1,200 and 1,450 m. Its overall extent of occurrence (EOO) is 172 km², however only 25% of this area contains forest habitat where the species may or may not occur. Its area of occupancy (AOO) is therefore estimated to be around 43 km². The main threat to the species is habitat loss and degradation as forest areas have been cleared for agriculture and housing, business and tourist developments. This has resulted in a severely fragmented population. Although harvesting of native trees greater than 10 cm diameter at breast height (dbh) is prohibited in Costa Rica, some illegal logging occurs and special permission to remove some trees under special circumstances can be obtained.

The total remaining population size is around 1,720 mature trees. Based on the ongoing threats affecting the species, it is inferred that the population has undergone a reduction of at least 95% over the past 180 years. With ongoing threats continuing to affect this species, it is likely that it will continue to decline in future. Currently it is not possible to give a reliable estimate of the rate of reduction projected over the next 100 years.

This tree’s restricted range in combination with severe fragmentation, continuing decline in habitat, AOO and population size; and small population size meets the thresholds and conditions for an Endangered listing. However, the inferred rate of past reduction (based on rate of past forest conversion and loss of primary forest cover) qualifies it for the higher category of Critically Endangered (CR A2ac).

Geographic Range [top]

Range Description:Ocotea monteverdensis is endemic to the Pacific slope of the Cordillera de Tilarán in Costa Rica. The core population is found between latitudes 10°25’N and 10°16’N and longitudes 84°42’W and 84°55’W (see Supplementary Material, Map 1: General distribution map with polygon delineating core extent). There it is generally limited to between 1,200 m and 1,500 m elevation (see Supplementary Material, Map 2: Topographic map delineating this elevation range within core extent). Three disjunct individuals have also been recorded from the Caribbean side of the Cordillera de Tilarán between 750 and 840 m (between 84°36’W and 84°51’W and latitudes 10°12’N and 10°25’N) (www.tropicos.org; Haber et al. 2000).

Outside this portion of the Cordillera de Tilarán, a single individual has been reported from 75 km to the northwest, on the Pacific slope of the Rincon de la Vieja volcano within the Cordillera de Guanacaste, Costa Rica (10°48’N, 85°20’24”W). This volcano is one of five comprising the Cordillera de Guanacaste; each volcano has narrow bands of Pacific slope life zones comparable to that of the species core range. It is certainly conceivable that small disjunct populations exist in yet to be surveyed areas at appropriate elevations of the Pacific slope of these volcanoes (www.tropicos.org).

Ocotea monteverdensis has seldom been seen outside the principal extent of occurrence (EOO) described here. In the three cases recorded from the Caribbean side, we hypothesize that Three-wattled Bellbirds (Procnias tricarunculatus) may have regurgitated occasional seeds to these locations during migratory movements over these areas, and these trees subsequently germinated and survived. Normally bellbirds fly short distances (<100 m) away from the wild avocado trees upon which they feed; after a subsequent 30 minutes or so of digestion, they regurgitate the seeds (Wenny and Levey 1998). During migration, the occasional bird could fly considerably greater distances after eating a fruit. We strongly suspect that such widely dispersed individuals are not located in a position for successful cross-fertilization to occur and do not represent members of a viable subpopulation. More intensive surveys are needed in these locations to search for disjunct subpopulations. The widely disjunct individual reported from Rincon de la Vieja Volcano could be a misidentification, as members of the genus Ocotea are notoriously difficult to identify and separate (see Van der Werff 2002). That region also deserves further inspection.

Population [top]

Population:There has been no systematic attempt to estimate the size of the population of mature trees of O. monteverdensis. Our attempt to do so here depends on a number of assumptions and estimates. From mapping the extent of occurrence (EOO) and the area of occupancy (AOO) of the species (Maps 1 and 2 in Supplementary Material), we estimate that the EOO (not including possible small disjunct populations outside this extent) is 172 km² and that approximately 25% of that remains in forest, which may or may not contain mature reproductive individuals (see Map 2 in Supplementary Material). The majority of this remaining forest is secondary re-growth that is dominated by fast-growing tree species. There are virtually no mature reproductive O. monteverdensis individuals in this regrowth, which followed harvesting of valuable timber during the 1955–1985 period. In many cases, though, there may be a number of saplings and seedlings of O. monteverdensis in the understorey. So we estimate that 20% of the remaining forest (i.e., 5% of the EOO) has a species composition similar to the primary forest; however, even these areas have had some valuable timber removed. In these “primary forest” areas, we estimate that the density of mature individuals of O. monteverdensis is two per ha. By these means we calculate:

172 km² x 0.05 (fraction “primary” forest) x 100 ha/km² x 2 mature trees/ha = 1,720 mature trees

The density of reproductive mature individuals deserves further measurement and documentation. We estimate that there are two mature individuals per hectare of appropriate forest within its range based upon our recent research into the phenology of flowering and fruiting in the Lauraceae family (in this elevation zone within the primary AOO of O. monteverdensis), and consumption of fruit by Three-wattled Bellbirds (Hamilton et al. in preparation). The 24 plots set up for this study (20 m by 50 m)—all within the EOO—contained mature O. monteverdensis at an average density of 4.1 per hectare. Since our plots were selected to be in areas where bellbirds were known to feed in most years—and since the fruit of O. monteverdensis is highly favoured by bellbirds—we assumed that these density numbers were positively biased. For this assessment we lowered the density of mature individuals to 2 trees per ha, given the assumed bias. Again, it is truly an estimate that needs further investigation. Probably the best method of obtaining accurate density numbers for mature trees (not to mention occupied habitat) would be to examine aerial photos taken during flowering—photos that do not exist to our knowledge; O. monteverdensis exhibits distinctive crowns when in full flower. We do have data that could be used to estimate the % of flowering individuals that fail to fruit.

The range of elevations containing potential habitat is delineated in Map 2 (see Supplementary Material). This map suggests that the greatest possible land surface area occupied by this species within that area—primarily the forested areas in green—is approximately 25% of the land surface. Not indicated on this map are numerous forest patches that are too small to delineate. Of course, as we have noted, most of this forested land surface is secondary growth which does not contain mature individuals of O. monteverdensis.

The current population of mature individuals is deteriorating because of the continued loss of forest, and the increasing fragmentation of that forest, due to housing and business development, as the population of Cañitas/Santa Elena/Cerro Plano/Monteverde continues to expand (Guindon 1988, 1996; Powell and Bjork 1994; Schelhas 1996; Harvey and Haber 1999; Harvey et al. 2000). In addition, natural replacement rates in remaining forests appears slow, as this species does not produce fruit until it is at least 25 years old (see the Habitats & Ecology section). In secondary forests recovering from logging or other disturbance, this species has strong competition from faster-growing species (e.g. Conostegia spp., Piper spp., Hampea appendiculata, Heliocarpus americanus, Croton mexicanus, Eugenia spp., etc.) (Haber et al. 2000). Natural direct reforestation of pastures adjacent to forests by large-seeded trees in the tropics (average O. monteverdensis seed diameter = 18.5 mm, length 29.3 mm) is generally totally ineffective (Nepstad et al. 1996).

Fragmentation: The population of O. monteverdensis may be considered “severely fragmented” based on the following: More than 50% of the total AOO is in patches too small to maintain a viable population (< 2 ha) and most of those patches are separated by more than 200 m. Given that the average dispersal distance achieved by birds that disperse this species is between 20 and 30 m, we think there is a risk of biological isolation for this species. The mean dispersal distance of 20–30 m is based on data on seed dispersal by birds for a closely related species (Ocotea endresiana) growing in the same region (Monteverde cloud forest) (Wenny and Levey 1998). This Ocotea species is dispersed by a nearly identical cohort of bird species as Ocotea monteverdensis (Keel-billed Toucan (Ramphastos sulfuratus), Black Guan (Chamaepetes unicolor), Three-wattled Bellbird (Procnias tricarunculatus), and Resplendent Quetzal (Pharomachrus mocinno).  [It is worth noting that the Vulnerable (BirdLife International 2012) Three-wattled Bellbird disperses seed to a greater average distance (~ 60 m) than all the other bird species (~ 12 m).]

Habitat and Ecology [top]

Habitat and Ecology:Ocotea monteverdensis is found in the premontane wet forest life zone that occurs between 800 and 1,500 meters of the northern Pacific slope of Costa Rica. This species is largely restricted to the more moist portions of this zone (1,200–1,450 m) (Tropicos.org 2013 (www.tropicos.org), Haber et al. 2000, Gonzalez and Poveda 2007). Map 3 (see Supplementary Material) shows the distribution of life zones in the Monteverde region, with documented O. monteverdensis locations delineated. Almost all points delineated are within the premontane wet forest life zone, or on its border. Only the three disjunct individuals outside of the primary area of occupancy are completely outside this life zone (Map 3).

Forests here are mostly evergreen and the climate is seasonally dry. While the area receives 2–2.5 meters of rain per year, 93% of that falls between May and December; during the four driest months (January–March), only 16 cm of precipitation falls on average (Stiles and Skutch 1989). Mature forest in this zone has an overstory of 30–40 m with an open understory populated by saplings, seedlings, ferns, and large-leaved perennials (Haber 2000, Haber et al. 2000).

This species regenerates from seed best under shade in moist organic soil, with minimal competition from ferns and large-leaved perennials. Saplings benefit from partial shade conditions (as opposed to full shade) as long as that does not greatly increase competition from faster-growing species. Ocotea monteverdensis can eventually reach a height of 35 m and thrives as a co-dominant or dominant canopy tree (Gonzalez and Poveda 2007). Fruit production has only been observed in any quantity, from trees greater than 50 cm diameter at breast height (dbh) and in dominant canopy positions (Hamilton et al. in prep). We observed flowering and fruit production in 13 adults for 6 years. Ninety-eight percent of the fruit produced came from the nine trees over 50 cm in dbh, all dominant  trees > 30 m in height. Three of the four trees less than this size (16–32 cm) never flowered or produced fruit, while the fourth (24 cm) produced a few fruit in one year out of six (Hamilton et al. in prep). As height growth in young trees under optimal conditions averages less than 1 m per year, and diameter growth no more than 2 cm per year, we estimate that individuals of this species require at least 25 years to become  mature reproductive trees (Joslin, Haber and Hamilton unpublished data). Natural dispersal of seed is achieved by frugivorous birds with large gapes (Wheelwright 1985, Mazer and Wheelwright 1993). The principal dispersers are the Keel-billed Toucan (Ramphastos sulfuratus), Emerald Toucanet (Aulacorhynchus prasinus), Black Guan (Chamaepetes unicolor), Three-wattled Bellbird (Procnias tricarunculatus), and the Resplendent Quetzal (Pharomachrus mocinno) (Wheelwright 1983, 1985; Wheelwright et al. 1984; Guindon 1997; Hamilton et al. in prep). Two of these species are Near Threatened (Black Guan, Resplendent Quetzal; Birdlife International 2012 in IUCN 2013), and a third is threatened (Vulnerable) (Three-wattled Bellbird; Birdlife International 2012 in IUCN 2013). In August 2011, O. monteverdensis in the Monteverde community was the focus of two weeks of intense fruit-feeding by a population of about 20 Oilbirds (Steatornis caripensis); this was the first record of resident Oilbirds in Costa Rica. As of August 2013, there are numerous Oilbirds in the Monteverde zone currently feeding on O. monteverdensis fruit.

Use and Trade [top]

Use and Trade: Ocotea monteverdensis historically has been a valuable timber tree, with lumber used principally for houses and hotels. The wood of Ocotea monteverdensis, like many of the Lauraceae in this region, is prized for use in interior building construction (floors, walls, ceilings).

Currently, by Costa Rican law, it is illegal to harvest native trees greater than 10 cm dbh without permission from the Ministry of Environment and Energy (MINAE). Permissions are sometimes obtained when trees threaten buildings, people, or grazing cows. Lumber obtained from permitted felling may only be used legally on the property where it was standing (e.g., for a new house, hotel, or cabina). Illegal logging of this species does occur but is no longer common. Shipping of lumber out of the immediate region is believed to be rare.

Threats [top]

Major Threat(s): By far the greatest threats to this species have been, and continue to be, the clearing of forests and forest patches, the fragmentation of remaining forest, and harvesting (often illegal) of individual trees for lumber. During the 1950s, ‘60s, ‘70s, and 80s much of the forest in the zone was cleared, initially for dairy farming pasture (Guindon 1997, Burlingame 2000). Some of that pastureland has since been converted to coffee plantations, housing developments, and businesses. For the past three decades, development of housing projects and tourist establishments has steadily encroached upon forests and remnant forest patches around the towns of La Cruz, Cañitas, Santa Elena, Cerro Plano, San Luis, and Monteverde (Map 2 in Supplementary Material). The population of Santa Elena/Monteverde area grew from 400 in 1984 (Porras and Miranda 2005) to 4,600 in 2006 (Acuña et al. 2006). Eighty per cent of the hotels, restaurants, and businesses in the area have been built since 1990 (Lind 2009). A single hotel and one restaurant existed in the area during the 1950s (Burlingame 2000); by 2007 there were 45 hotels and 62 restaurants (Welch 2008). The current annual rate of population growth is estimated to be 3.8% (Welch 2008).

Another pressure on the remaining forest land comes from the heavy use of trails and access roads for hikers, nature-lovers, horseback riders, zip-lines, etc. Eighty-five percent of businesses in this region are considered to be tourism-related (Amador 2004).  The number of tourists visiting the area has increased from 15,000 per year in the 1980s to 200,000 per year in 2003 (Dallas 2005; Tropical Science Center 2007a, 2007b; Welch 2008).

Almost none of the distribution area of O. monteverdensis receives permanent protection by conservation easements or government ownership. Map 1 (see Supplementary Material) shows large areas of protected land in the region, occupied chiefly by the Monteverde Cloud Forest Preserve, the Children’s Eternal Rainforest, and the San Ramon Reserve, almost all of which are on the Caribbean side of the continental divide. However, virtually all of the primary area occupied by this species falls outside of these reserves, on the Pacific side of the continental divide (Map 1 in the Supplementary Material).

Logging of trees of native species greater than 10 cm dbh is prohibited by Costa Rican law; however, some illegal logging occurs, and permission to remove trees for special purposes can be obtained. Local residents frequently use existing forest on private land to obtain wood for posts and firewood, posing a threat to regenerating saplings.

Additional threats are climate-related. This mountainous region experiences strong winds, particularly during December and January.  Forest fragmentation increases exposure to wind, especially for dominant and super-dominant trees like O. monteverdensis. Global climate change poses an additional unpredictable threat. Rainfall amounts in recent years have been particularly unpredictable, resulting in excessive rain some years and record droughts in others.

Threats from native plants (in addition to general competition) come from Strangler figs (Ficus tuerckheimii) and various species of mistletoe (Psittacanthus spp). The stranglers are epiphytes that often result in the death of large, mature canopy trees by gradually overtopping them. The parasitic mistletoes also frequently kill large trees (local name means "kill tree").

Conservation Actions [top]

Conservation Actions: Conservation actions relating to O. monteverdensis are primarily of two types—(a) protection of existing forests, forest patches, and individual trees and (b) reforestation of disturbed areas (chiefly pastures) with seedlings. The one permanently protected area (Bajo del Tigre reserve, part of the Children’s Eternal Rainforest) that is within the EOO is very small, totaling about 40 ha and almost entirely in secondary and high-graded forest. One extensive tract of 75 ha of largely primary forest (Monteverde Curi-Cancha Wildlife Refuge) is currently being managed as a public tourist attraction for wildlife viewing. A few other smaller areas of mostly primary forest (from 1 to 20 ha in size) are managed for night-time wildlife viewing or as scenic trails for hotel guests. These areas are currently protected by their owners (in large part for profit), but they have no long-term protection. Fortunately the Costa Rican government now has laws that forbid cutting of native species of trees larger than 10 cm dbh without permission of the Ministry of Environment, and Energy of Costa Rica (MINAE). Also, forested buffer zones on the sides of perennial streams are protected by law. Many of the remaining mature individuals are growing within these streamside buffers.

Since 1998, the Costa Rican Conservation Foundation (Fundación Conservacionista Costarricense) has conducted a reforestation campaign within the greater Monteverde zone. The goal has been to reforest pastureland and other disturbed land with trees approximating the composition of the original forest. Tree seedlings grown from locally-collected seeds have been distributed to farmers and/or planted by volunteer groups. Over the past 14 years, over 110,000 trees have been distributed from their Los Llanos tree nursery (over 150,000 trees distributed when including their collaborative nursery operated by the University of Georgia, U.S.A.) on a minimum of 275 hectares. Percent composition of seedlings that were O. monteverdensis is 6.1% (7,100 trees). The percent survival of O. monteverdensis ranges from 21.2% (n=500) to 48% (n=110) with a weighted average of 26.4%. Ocotea monteverdensis seedlings do not survive when planted lower than the premontane wet life zone (0% survival of 230 planted seedlings in two different years). The trees do survive in moderate sun/partial shade (small pastures) and grow to heights of 2+ meters (average 199.5 cm) by their 4th year planted in the ground (after growing for 9 months in plastic bags in the tree nursery before planting). The highest rate of growth (from 1 to 5 years) has been observed during their third year in the ground (approximately 0.5 m). Anecdotal evidence indicates that protection from desiccation by wind and direct sun is of utmost importance; such exposure to desiccation appears to be the largest detriment to seedling survival of O. monteverdensis within the premontane wet forest life zone (D. Hamilton, pers. comm).

Conservation actions needed:
  • Tree stand improvement to release existing seedlings and saplings from excessive competition.
  • Planting seedlings in areas of appropriate climate, without excessive sun and wind exposure.
  • Better (or continuing) protection of remaining reproducing trees.
  • Education of landowners who have remnant mature individuals and/or regenerating areas.
