Taxonomy [top]

Kingdom Phylum Class Order Family
Animalia Arthropoda Insecta Hemiptera Psyllidae

Scientific Name: Acizzia mccarthyi
Species Authority: Taylor & Moir, 2014
Common Name(s):
English – McCarthy’s Plant-louse
Taxonomic Source(s): Taylor, G.S. and Moir, M.L. 2014. Further evidence of the coextinction threat for jumping plant-lice: three new Acizzia (Psyllidae) and Trioza (Triozidae) from Western Australia. Insect systematics & evolution 45(3): 283-302.
Taxonomic Notes: Acizzia mccarthyi has recently been described (Taylor & Moir, 2014). No other names exist for the species and the taxonomy has not been questioned.

Assessment Information [top]

Red List Category & Criteria: Endangered B1ab(i,ii,iii,iv)+2ab(i,ii,iii,iv) ver 3.1
Year Published: 2015
Date Assessed: 2014-12-03
Assessor(s): Moir, M. & Leng, M.C.
Reviewer(s): Gerlach, J.
Justification:
Acizzia mccarthyi is particularly at risk of extinction as its likely host breadth is very low (one host plant species; Moir et al. 2011). We have used the host-breadth models of Vesk et al. (2010), which assess host breadth in light of uncertainty (such as the potential of the insect to be a tourist and not actually feeding on any given plant species that it is found on), to examine this. Coupled with the low dispersal ability, the probability of extinction is high.

This species is assessed as Endangered based on criterion B. It has an potential extent of occurrence (EOO) of 148 km2, a potential area of occupancy (AOO) of 36 km2, it occurs at two locations and the population is severely fragmented. There are inferred continuing declines in EOO, AOO, habitat and the number of subpopulations.

Geographic Range [top]

Range Description:Acizzia mccarthyi has been recorded from two subpopulations in the Stirling Range National Park (SRNP), which is in the southwest biodiversity hotspot of Western Australia. The specific localities are Mt Talyuberlup and Paper Collar Creek (also known as Papa Colla Creek).

The area of occupancy (AOO), calculated using a 2 km x 2 km grid, of the known subpopulations of Acizzia mccarthyi is 8 km2. However, a number of the host plant (Acacia veronica) subpopulations have not yet been surveyed and the species may occur in a number of these. The potential AOO and extent of occurrence (EOO), based on occurrence in all unsurveyed host plant subpopulations, are 36 km2 and 148 km2, respectively. These higher potential EOO and AOO values are used to inform the final category and criteria. The two subpopulations represent two threat-based locations.

Population [top]

Population:Numbers of Acizzia mccarthyi have not been counted as this is hard to achieve due their small size and rarity. The distribution of A. mccarthyi is severely fragmented due to its very narrow host-breath association with its host plant Acacia veronica. As there are no estimates of population size it is not known if there has been any reduction in numbers. A 2007 survey from 60 host plants yielded 28 individuals (M. Moir unpubl. data). There were likely to be more individuals on each plant than captured as the sampling methods (beating and vacuuming) could not reach the top of taller host plants.

To date there are 13 known host plant subpopulations of A. veronica residing in the SRNP, having a total combined population area of <10 km2 (Taylor and Moir 2014). Surveys in 2007-2008 resulted in three A. veronica subpopulations not located and one new subpopulation discovered. From the ten previously known A. veronica subpopulation, six were sampled and A. mccarthyi was found to be present at two (the known host plant subpopulations of A. mccarthyi, occur in an area less than <10 km2). Given the distance to the nearest known host subpopulation (c. 20 km), A. mccarthyi  recolonization of new sites is highly unlikely.

Habitat and Ecology [top]

Habitat and Ecology:Acizzia mccarthyi is closely associated with its only known host plant Acacia veronica, which has a very restricted distribution; it is the only known Acacia species to reside solely in the SRNP, Western Australia (Maslin 1989). Acacia veronica has a Western Australian conservation listing of ‘Priority 3’. It is a small, often spindly, tree (1.5-7 m) restricted mainly to watercourses in the SRNP, with smaller individual plants growing at sheltered higher altitudes, often associated with Marri (Corymbia calophylla) woodlands (Maslin 1989, Taylor and Moir 2009).

Acizzia mccarthyi nymphs and adult feed on phloem of their host plant during spring, and potentially, summer. Most species of plant-lice have three discrete generations per year in temperate Australia. Adults are likely to occur in spring (September-October), summer (December-January) and late summer/autumn (March-May) respectively. Eggs and nymphs are likely to occur at most other times of the year. However, the exact life-cycle and timing is unknown for A. mccarthyi.

Like most Psyllidae, eggs of A. mccarthyi would most likely be attached to the host plant, and nymphs would remain on its host throughout development. Adults are relatively sedentary with often colonial lifestyles and may disperse short distances (c. 1 km) to adjacent host plants but are unlikely to move far from the host plant patch. Evidence of this lack of dispersal capability is given by four unoccupied host plant patches of A. veronica that occur within 10 km of an occupied patch.

Threats [top]

Major Threat(s): Acizzia mccarthyi is closely associated with its only known host plant, Acacia veronica. Therefore, threats to A. veronica subpopulations such as climate change, habitat/land clearing, fragmentation and inappropriate fire regime indirectly threaten A. mccarthyi.

Climate change (current and future threat)
Gully habitats of A. mccarthyi in the SRNP are at risk of drying out which may cause A. mccarthyi subpopulations to decline or go locally extinct due to reduced rainfall to sustain host plant subpopulations.

Habitat/land clearing (past, current and future threat)
Clearing is a threat despite host plant subpopulations being confined to a National Park. One subpopulation occurs near a road and around a car park, so road works could remove or injure plant individuals. This could directly impact A. mccarthyi through the loss of host plants. Clearing may also cause habitat degradation and cause direct mortality for A. mccarthyi subpopulations.

Fragmentation (past, current and future threat)
Host plant subpopulations in the SRNP are severely fragmented. Both known subpopulations are too far apart (c. 20 km) for A. mccarthyi to recolonise naturally if A. mccarthyi goes locally extinct in one subpopulation. This will cause direct mortality for A. marcarthyi subpopulations with no natural recolonisation. Therefore, even if the host plant subpopulation restores itself (e.g., after fire), it is possible for the permanent loss of a subpopulation of A. mccarthyi.

Wildfires and inappropriate fire regimes (past, current and future threat)
All host plant subpopulations (and therefore A. mccarthyi subpopulations) are at risk of wildfires. Fire could directly extinguish one subpopulation of A. mccarthyi if the entire subpopulation of A. veronica is burnt. Indirectly, fire could cause the local extinction of A mccarthyi if no above ground plants are available for surviving bugs. Natural recolonisation is unlikely to occur due to fragmented nature of A. mccarthyi subpopulations (see above).

Conservation Actions [top]

Conservation Actions: Acizzia mccarthyi has been listed by the Western Australian State government as a threatened species under the category of ‘Vulnerable’ in 2012.

To date no areas are actively managed for A. mccarthyi in the SRNP. Acizzia mccarthyi should benefit directly from the management of subpopulations of its host plant A. veronica as, to date, it has only been recorded from this plant host (Taylor and Moir 2014). To ensure the survival and recovery of A. mccarthyi, there is a need to protect and manage their host plant A. veronica from threats such as land clearing, inappropriate fire regimes, fragmentation and, due to the gully habitats the plant and plant-louse prefer, potentially climate change.
