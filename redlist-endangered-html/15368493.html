Kingdom Phylum Class Order Family
Plantae Tracheophyta Magnoliopsida Asterales Compositae

Scientific Name: Lipotriche tithonioides
Species Authority: (Aké Assi) D.J.N.Hind
Common Name(s):
English – Simandou Daisy
French – Marguerite de Simandou
Synonym(s):
Melanthera tithonioides Aké Assi
Taxonomic Source(s): Hind, D.J.N. 2014. A synopsis of the African genus Lipotriche (Compositae: Heliantheae: Ecliptinae). Kew Bulletin 69(3): 1-14. DOI: 10.1007/s12225-014-9528-7].
Taxonomic Notes: Wild (1965) reduced the species to a synonym of M. scandens subsp. dregei without any basis according to Compositae specialist Dr. D.J.N. Hind (pers. comm. 2005).

African Melanthera species differ from those of the Neotropics in several features and have been transferred by Dr. Hind to a separate genus, Lipotriche (Hind 2014).

Assessment Information [top]

Red List Category & Criteria: Endangered B1ab(i,ii,iii,iv,v)+2ab(i,ii,iii,iv,v) ver 3.1
Year Published: 2015
Date Assessed: 2014-03-06
Assessor(s): Cheek, M.
Reviewer(s): Hilton-Taylor, C.
Facilitator/Compiler(s): Harvey, Y. & Baker, F.
Justification:

Lipotriche tithonioides is assessed as Endangered B1ab(i,ii, iii,iv, v)+2ab(i,ii,iii,iv,v) since only five threat based locations are known, at two of which it is known to be threatened. At a third and fourth location these is no evidence of any threats. No data are available at the fifth location regarding threats. The area of occupancy (AOO) is estimated as 60 km2 using the 4 km2 cells advocated by IUCN (2012). The extent of occurrence (EOO) is assessed as 1,300 (1,293.48) km2.

Currently there is no evidence of decline of this species at its Pic de Fon location where it has clearly increased due to controlled disturbance of its habitat. However within a few years this may change when a potential mining operation is developed, this would effectively remove the location and result in the loss of thousands of individuals, potentially most of the global population, since the numbers of individuals known at S Foko, Dabatini and Bero locations are far lower. However it is not possible to calculate the projected decline since the Mt. Tonkoui location is not accessible and numbers of individuals unknown.

The species was first discovered from Mt. Tonkoui in Côte d'Ivoire by Aké Assi (1964), whence numerous specimens derive, possibly indicating that it is frequent and common there. In 2005 the species was discovered at Pic de Fon in the Simandou Range of Guinea where it grows abundantly at many sites, with many thousands of individuals. Limited disturbance of its habitat favours increase in the populations (M. Cheek pers. obs. 2005-2007).

Extensive searches throughout the Loma-Man Mts within Guinea for this species were conducted 2007-2012, resulting in its discovery at Mt. Bero (between Simandou and Nimba Ranges) by P.K. Haba, P.M. Haba, F. Fofana and A. Traore in 2008. Elsewhere it is absent, despite apparently identical habitat being available.

Habitat and Ecology [top]

Habitat and Ecology:Lipotriche tithonioides occurs at the interface between submontane forest and submontane grassland. Rarely is it found in forest gaps.Its lower altitudinal limit is 750 m, its upper altitudinal limit is 1,600 m asl (summit of Pic de Fon). It flowers late in the wet season, producing seed from October-January. From December-May the stems of this perennial partly die-back and are inconspicuous. In cultivation, with sufficient water, plants reach 3 m tall, and live for several years. The seeds lack plumes or spines, and thus the means to disperse by the usual Compositae vectors of wind or animals. Thus, even within the Simandou Range, the species does not occur in the Oueleba section (despite exhaustive searching) nor the Mt Tibé, nor northern (Kerouane) sections. Seed is produced abundantly and seedlings grow rapidly, apparently establishing new plants easily (M. Cheek pers. obs. 2005-2008). It is thought that the species is not favoured by intense fires, cattle trampling or lowland conditions. Nor can it withstand forest shade. The generation length of this species is not known. Plants are thought to reach flowering from seed in 1-3 years and to flower over several years, before senescing, but this needs confirmation.

Use and Trade [top]

Use and Trade: There is no known use for this species.

Threats [top]

Major Threat(s): Since the type location, at Mt. Tonkoui, Côte d'Ivoire, is inaccessible for reasons of politics and security, it is not possible to assess threats there. No threats are known at the locations south of Foko Hill and northwest of Mt. Dabatini in the Simandou Range of Guinea. At Mt. Bero, trampling by the high number of introduced Zebu cattle is damaging the individuals there (P. Haba pers. comm.). At Pic de Fon in the Simandou Range, the entire subpopulation is threatened by the potential development of an iron ore mine that is planned in the next few years.

Conservation Actions [top]

Conservation Actions: More than 10,000 seeds of this species from Pic de Fon have been seedbanked in Guinea and at the Millennium Seed Bank (MSB) at Wakehurst, Surrey, UK (RBG Kew). A conservation poster promoting the importance and protection of this species was widely distributed within Guinea in 2008, similarly, packets of seed of the species were distributed within Guinea to promote its cultivation as an ornamental plant, although this was not successful. Translocation of this species to a new location, within its natural range, in accordance with IUCN rulings, was initiated by the author in 2008 at the Bronkedou Mts, as a benign introduction (now termed an "assisted colonization"). The survival and gradual increase of this species at that location has been monitored by P.K. Haba in subsequent years and is expected to result in proof of the establishment of three successive generations within the next 1-2 years.
