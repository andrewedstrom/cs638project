Taxonomy [top]

Kingdom Phylum Class Order Family
Plantae Tracheophyta Liliopsida Liliales Amaryllidaceae

Scientific Name: Caliphruria hartwegiana
Species Authority: Herb.
Synonym(s):
Urceolina hartwegiana (Herb.) Traub

Assessment Information [top]

Red List Category & Criteria: Endangered B2ab(iii) ver 3.1
Year Published: 2013
Date Assessed: 2008-05-21
Assessor(s): Crook, V.
Reviewer(s): Scott, J.A.
Justification:
Caliphruria hartwegiana is currently only known from five collections in the Magdalena Valley montane forest region of Colombia. The extent of occurrence (EOO) and area of occupancy (AOO) of the species were calculated as 6,695 and 20 km2 (five grid cells of 2x2 km2) respectively. Combined with the low number of locations currently known for the species (four), the fragmentation of remaining forest fragments in the region and the continuous decline in habitat occurring due to agriculture, over-grazing and plantations, a rating of Endangered has been maintained here.

The species was collected from the private Finca Merenberg Reserve in 1975 and 1984, and if this subpopulation is still present, is likely to be well protected. Without protection within private reserves such as these, species such as C. hartwegiana may become critically endangered in the near future, especially if harvesting from the wild is occurring to any extent, being an additional threat to the species. It is recommended, therefore, that the reserve be searched for this species as soon as possible, and if no plants are found that a rating of Critically Endangered is applied immediately.

Geographic Range [top]

Range Description:This species is known exclusively from the Rio Magdalena Valley in western-central Colombia. It is known from only five collections, two from the department of Cundinamarca (Caparrapi; near Guaduas) and three from the department of Huila (Rio Magdalena near Paicol; forest around Finca Mehrenberg; Finca Merenberg/Vereda Agua Bonita).

NOTE: Finca Merenberg is a private reserve where significant scientific research has been carried out in the past, especially plant and bird studies. Population [top]

Population:Herbarium specimens were collected between 1882 and 1984. Meerow (1989) describes this species as "rare" and "seldom collected". At present, there appear to be two subpopulations, separated by 300 km, one in Cundimarca and the other in Huila. As this is such a poorly collected species, absence in suitable habitat between these two areas cannot be confirmed. The Magdalena Valley montane forest ecoregion spans the mountain for over 700 km, with patches ranging from 20 to 100 km wide.

Meerow (1989) states: "The rarity of Eucharis and Caliphruria species throughout their range is a striking characteristic of their distribution. Single, widely dispersed clumps of bulbs are more the rule than the exception. Herbarium collections are usually unicates and often indicate the relative infrequency with which the plants were encountered in the forest".
Current Population Trend:Unknown

Habitat and Ecology [top]

Habitat and Ecology:Found in the understory of montane forests of the Rio Magdalena Valley, 600-2,350 m asl. Meerow (1989) highlights the fact that Eucharis or Caliphruria species are nowhere abundant, and they are found growing only in primary, rarely secondary, forest on soils of high fertility. Caliphruria is a small genus closely allied to Eucharis. It comprises four species, namely C. korsakoffii, C. hartwegiana, C. subedentata and C. tenera. With the exception of C. korsakoffii which is native to northern Peru, the other three species are native to low montane forests in Colombia. Caliphruria hartwegiana is distinguished from C. subdentata by its smaller flowers and short stamens with long teeth interposed between the filaments.
Systems:Terrestrial

Use and Trade [top]

Use and Trade: Caliphruria species are prized for their highly ornamental dark glossy foliage. This species is probably the most commonly cultivated species of the genus, however it is also known to be cultivated, with several cultivated dry specimens stored at Kew, and three plants growing in the Kew Palm House. These three plants were derived from 26 plants sent to Topher Martin, Syon Park, Isleworth on 06/10/1998, however it is unclear where these originally derived from. The level of collection of this species from the wild is unknown.

Labels of a number of herbarium specimens of Eucharis species indicate that lowland native people collect the bulbs for use as poultices. The bulbs are mashed, heated and applied to sores and tumours. Most local inhabitants whom Meerow met while collecting Eucharis in the Orient were readily familiar with the plants when shown photographs. Caliphruria species may therefore be used in a similar way.

Threats [top]

Major Threat(s): Large scale deforestation has proven catastrophic to Eucharis and Caliphruria plants, which are unable to adapt to the higher light intensity of the clearings and soon perish (Meerow 1989). Meerow (1989) described this taxon as the most common species of Caliphruria, at one time found throughout the moist lower and middle montane forests of the slopes of the cordilleras central and west of 76°. Much of this forest type has been destroyed in western Colombia and the species is no longer abundant. In the course of fieldwork in 1984, Meerow (1989) was not able to find the species in its historical localities nor in the remnants of lower and middle montane forest that he explored. A similar situation is likely to occur for C. hartwegiana, although this species has generally been collected over 100 km further inland than C. subedentata.

The lower altitude subpopulations of this species were collected on the border between the Magdalena Valley montane forest and Magdalena Valley dry forest ecoregions, with the latter type having been seriously degraded. Agriculture, and overgrazing, especially from introduced goats, has destroyed much of the original habitat here. Few areas within the montane forest ecoregion are still in good condition due to large scale use of the slopes for coffee growing and farming, but also because more than 70% of the Colombian population lives in this region. There are several parks in the upper Magdalena basin, such as Guácharos, Puracé, Huila, Hermosas, Nevados, Picachos, Sumapaz and Chingaza, that conserve most of the high montane forests, mostly above 3,000 m asl, but unfortunately, below 2,000 m asl there are few conservation figures or initiatives, besides the purchasing of nature reserves by the civilian society with the aim to protect most of the remaining fragments of native forests. Even some of these private reserves, such as Finca Merenberg, are under threat — see conservation measures

Collection from the wild could also be threatening this species, with Caliphruria species being prized for their highly ornamental dark glossy foliage. However, harvesting levels, if any, are unknown.

Conservation Actions [top]

Conservation Actions: This species has been collected from the Finca Merenberg private reserve. This was founded in 1950 by the Buch family from ca 150 ha forest and ca 150 ha cattle pasture. Unfortunately, the 150 ha of forest are dwindling, partly owing to the creation of more pasture by the owners (second-generation Buch), and partly due to selective forest clearance of hundreds of mature oak and other hardwoods, which has drastically changed the forest physiognomy. If a subpopulation of this species still exists within the reserve (last collected there in 1984), it is likely to be more protected, however, than any subpopulations outside the reserve. Presence of a subpopulation of this species in the reserve needs to be confirmed as soon as possible.

One collection was made near to Guaduas, and could have been from within the Cuenca Hidrografica del Rio San Francisco National Protective Forest Reserve. Three plants are growing at the Palm House in the Royal Botanic Gardens, Kew — these were derived from a batch of 26 plants sent to Syon House, Isleworth, however it is unclear of their provenance.
