Taxonomy [top]

Kingdom Phylum Class Order Family
Animalia Chordata Actinopterygii Osmeriformes Galaxiidae

Scientific Name: Galaxias cobitinis
Species Authority: McDowall & Waters, 2002
Common Name(s):
English – Lowland Longjaw Galaxias
Taxonomic Notes: Although classified as one species, the two geographic populations have been ranked separately in the National listings carried out by the New Zealand Department of Conservation, as they are more genetically distinct and more divergent than some described sister species of galaxiids (Waters and Craw 2008). Taxonomic research is needed to determine whether this species should actually be split into two (R. Hitchmough pers. comm. 2011).

Assessment Information [top]

Red List Category & Criteria: Critically Endangered B2ac(iv) ver 3.1
Year Published: 2014
Date Assessed: 2014-07-30
Assessor(s): Allibone, R, West, D, Closs, G., Franklin, P., David, B., Ling, N. & Crow, S.
Reviewer(s): Hitchmough, R. & Gibson, C.
Facilitator/Compiler(s): Gibson, C. & Buley, K.
Justification:
The Lowland Longjaw Galaxias (Galaxias cobitinis) is endemic to the southern east coast of the South Island of New Zealand, where it has an extremely limited distribution. It occurs as two distinct sub-populations in two river catchments (including six sites in the Waitaki River Catchment and in the Kauru and Kakanui Rivers). The majority of the population of this species lives in the drought-prone Kauru River, which is a tributary of the Kakanui River. Although its original range is not known, it is expected to now only occupy a small fraction of its former distribution as a result of the impacts from introduced salmonid fish. The current Extent of Occurrence is calculated as 1,550 km2, however the current distribution of this species is severely fragmented and the total area of occupancy (AOO) is calculated as 2.4 km2. Sections of the Kauru River gravel-bed regularly dry up, reducing habitat to a series of isolated pools during severe drought. During these dry periods the fish survive in the groundwater zone, springs and remnant pools. This species is listed as 'Nationally Critical' based on a very small population size according to the New Zealand Department of Conservation's threat classification scheme. In June 2001, the population size in the Kauru River was estimated to be less than 250 mature individuals. Subsequent surveys indicate that the population of this species undergoes extreme natural fluctuations from just tens of individuals to 1000's due to variable climatic impacts (such as flooding and drying events). Ten years of monitoring data at the Kauru River have shown the greatest population fluctuations occur when long drought periods are followed by large floods. Such events can reduce the population by 90%. The populations in the Waitaki catchment are all thought to be small and contain few individuals. Predation of both adult and larval fishes by the introduced Brown Trout (Salmo trutta) is a threat to G. cobitinis.  During stable flow years, the Kauru River populations can expand rapidly indicating the predation by other species does not limit recovery in Kauru. However, in more stable springtime habitat that G. cobitinis occupies in the Waitaki catchment, predation by salmonids is a major threat. Other threats include gravel abstraction in the Kauru River, which affects the structure of stream bed and reducing the opportunities for the fish to access groundwater refuges, making them more susceptible to predation and drying events. Water abstraction also causes habitat reduction and increases the durations of dry periods in the Kauru River. The increasing frequency of severe climate fluctuations is expected to create additional pressure on the population. This species is assessed as Critically Endangered as the area of occupancy is 2.4 km2 and the population is severely fragmented with extreme fluctuations in the number of mature individuals.

Geographic Range [top]

Range Description:Galaxias cobitinis is endemic to the southern east coast of the South Island of New Zealand, where it has an extremely limited distribution (Department of Conservation 2004). It occurs as two distinct sub-populations in two river catchments (including six sites in the Waitaki River Catchment and in the Kauru and Kakanui Rivers) (McDowall and Waters 2002, Department of Conservation 2004). The majority of the population of this species lives in the drought-prone Kauru River, which is a tributary of the Kakanui River. Although its original range is not known, it is expected to now only occupy a small fraction of its former distribution as a result of the impacts from introduced salmonid fish. The current extent of occurrence (EOO) is calculated as 1,550 km2, however the current distribution of this species is severely fragmented and the total area of occupancy (AOO) is calculated as 2.4 km2 (NZFFD 2011; FENZ 2010). Area of occupancy was calculated from the sum of the lengths x the estimated stream widths of river reaches in which this species is recorded in the National New Zealand Freshwater Fish Database (NZFFD 2011). Extent of occurrence was calculated using an intersection of New Zealand Freshwater Fish database records and spatial representations (polygons) of Freshwater Ecosystems of New Zealand; 4th order river catchments (FENZ 2010, NZFFD 2011).

Population [top]

Population:This species is listed as 'Nationally Critical' based on a very small population size according to the New Zealand Department of Conservation's threat classification scheme (Goodman et al. 2014). In June 2001, the population size in the Kauru River was estimated to be less than 250 mature individuals (Department of Conservation 2004). Subsequent surveys indicate that the population of this species undergoes extreme natural fluctuations from just tens of individuals to 1,000's due to variable climatic impacts (such as flooding and drying events). Ten years of monitoring data at the Kauru River have shown the greatest population fluctuations occur when long drought periods are followed by large floods. Such events can reduce the population by 90%. During stable flow year, the population can expand rapidly indicating the predation by other species does not limit recovery in Kauru (Golder Associates, draft 2014). However, in more stable springtime habitat that G. cobitinis occupies in the Waitaki catchment, predation by salmonids is a major threat. The populations in the Waitaki catchment are all thought to be small and contain few individuals (Department of Conservation 2004).

Habitat and Ecology [top]

Habitat and Ecology:This species lives on the margins of shallow streams with riffles and loose gravel substrate in the toe slopes of the lowlands. It occurs in semi-permanent streams, where the fish have adapted to burrow into the substrate during dry periods (Dunn and O'Brien 2006). Sections of the Kauru River gravel-bed regularly dry up, reducing habitat to a series of isolated pools during severe drought (McDowall and Allibone 2004). During these dry periods the fish survive in the groundwater zone, springs and remnant pools. This is a very short lived species, with many individuals living just one year. Generation length is therefore one year. Most females will only spawn once (either in the spring or autumn and when females are about 55 mm in length). Maximum total length of this species is typically less than 90 mm (Dunn and O'Brien 2006). Fecundity has been recorded at about 70 eggs per female. Larvae rear in backwaters of the main channel. Spawning occurs in up-welling zones, deep within the gravels. Adults feed on invertebrate larvae from the rocks (McDowall and Waters 2002, Department of Conservation 2004).

Use and Trade [top]

Use and Trade: This species is not utilized.

Threats [top]

Major Threat(s): Predation of both adult and larval fishes by the introduced Brown Trout (Salmo trutta) is thought to be a threat to G. cobitinis. The combined effects of consecutive drought and floods cause very large reductions in the abundance of this species in the Kauru River. Additional issues include gravel abstraction in the Kauru River, which affects the structure of stream bed and reduces the opportunities for the fish to access groundwater refuges, making them more susceptible to predation and drying events. Water abstraction also causes habitat reduction and increases the durations of dry periods in the Kauru River. The increasing frequency of natural climate fluctuations is expected to create additional pressure on the population. However, the impact of the other threats is obscured by the magnitude of these climatically driven fluctuations (Golder Associates, draft 2014, Dunn and O'Brien 2006). The presence of the invasive diatom Didymosphenia geminata (which blankets the substrate and prevents burrowing) in the Kakanui River also presents a potentially significant threat to the Kauru River population; it is unknown how D. geminata would impact G. cobitinis should it establish in the Kauru River.

Conservation Actions [top]

Conservation Actions: Trout barriers have been built in three locations, which are accompanied by trout removal upstream of these barriers. Barrier inspections are carried out and monitoring occurs. Trout fishing has been stopped in the Kauru River to prevent the spread of the North American invasive diatom, Didymosphenia geminata. Advocacy is taking place to prevent instream activities (such as gravel and water abstraction), which impact this species (McDowall and Waters 2002).
