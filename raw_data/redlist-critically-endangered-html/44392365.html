Taxonomy [top]

Kingdom Phylum Class Order Family
Plantae Tracheophyta Liliopsida Bromeliales Bromeliaceae

Scientific Name: Cryptanthus diamantinensis
Species Authority: Leme

Assessment Information [top]

Red List Category & Criteria: Critically Endangered B1ab(iii)+2ab(iii) ver 3.1
Year Published: 2014
Date Assessed: 2009-02-13
Assessor(s): Romand-Monnier, F.
Reviewer(s): Moraes, M.
Justification:
Cryptanthus diamantinensis is a saxicolous, stoloniferous, spreading bromeliad, growing on reasonably flat rocky surfaces, below Caatinga vegetation, in the lowlands of the Chapada Diamantina, in Bahia. The taxon has only been collected and described recently and appears to have a very restricted geographic distribution as it is currently only known from the type locality. The area of occupancy (AOO) and the extent of occurrence (EOO) are 10 km2 and 50 km2 respectively. There are numerous threats to plant species in the region (such as agricultural expansion and timber extraction) and other saxicolous Brazilian bromeliads have been found to be directly threatened by fires and collection for the horticultural trade. C. diamantinensis was collected within the Chapada Diamantina National Park, however, it is unknown how much protection this actually offers to the species. Caatinga vegetation has been extensively converted and degraded. However, a large part remains poorly explored and surveyed. Given that the taxon is only known from one location, has high habitat specificity, and given the remaining high level of threats and likely current inadequate protection, a precautionary rating of Critically Endangered is given to this species. In the Atlantic Forest, the fact that many bromeliad species are known from only one population is not due to uneven sampling effort but because they represent the last remnants of an earlier biodiversity (Filho and Leme 2006).

Geographic Range [top]

Range Description:This species is a recently collected and described, saxicolous bromeliad, which is endemic to the Chapada Diamentina (plateau), in the northeastern Brazilian state of Bahia. A second specimens was found in 2000 (Thomas et al. 12272) located in the Mata de Cipo, however, the determination is not confirmed. The extent of occurrence (EOO) is estimated as 50 km2 and the area of occupancy (AOO) is 8-10 km2.

Habitat and Ecology [top]

Habitat and Ecology:This species is a saxicolous bromeliad, growing on reasonably flat rocky surfaces, below the typical Caatinga vegetation (Leme 1999). Caatinga is found on lowlands of the Chapada Diamantina, principally under 700 m asl (Parrini et al. 1999). Caatinga is a mosaic of thorn scrub and seasonally dry tropical forests landscapes found in semi arid eastern Brazil. The tall Caatinga forests are now scarce and typical Caatinga vegetation consists principally of open scrub where xerophytic species predominate. C. diamantinensis (ecologival type one; Benzing 2000) is a spreading, mat forming saxicolous bromeliad, which is likely to possess specific morphophysiological adaptations to withstand the harsh edaphic and microclimatic conditions of soil islands of rock surfaces of the Caatinga. Vegetative propagation is likely to be an advantage; however, the population may have a reduced genetic diversity (Conceiao et al. 2007). A high number of Cryptanthus species are found in Bahia. Cryptanthus species depend on their roots for water supply as they do not store water in their center. The fleshy fruits are dispersed by animals. The fruits contain more seeds than in other Cryptanthus species (Benzing 2000). Cryptanthus species are generally pollinated by insects (Benzing 2000).
Systems:Terrestrial
Continuing decline in area, extent and/or quality of habitat:Yes

Use and Trade [top]

Use and Trade: Plants are grown as ornamentals.

Threats [top]

Major Threat(s): Caatinga is the largest dry forest region in South America and certainly one of the richest dry forests in the world. This forest originally covered ca. 70% of the vegetative cover of northeast Brazil. At least 50% of the Caatinga has been either completely converted from its native vegetation or modified in a major way by human activities (i.e., the second/third most degraded ecosystem in Brazil) (Da Silva 2001, Leal et al. 2005). Vegetation remnants are distributed in different sized fragments. The locality of the type collection is today a mosaic of cropland, shrub and herbaceous cover, of low human footprint (Sanderson et al. 2002, GIS data). Parrini et al. (1999) reports that areas of pristine vegetation still occur within the Chapada Diamantina, however, a high proportion of the Caatinga is under high human pressure. Unsustainable timber extraction for fuel, clearance for pastures, overgrazing, slash and burn agriculture, "Sertanjos" (conversion of remnant vegetation to new and short lived crops), desertification, extensive and uncontrolled fires, urban sprawl and settlements and, more recently, cotton and sugar cane cultivation are all playing critical roles in the near complete destruction of these important regional ecosystems (Parrini et al. 1999, Da Silva 2001).

Extensive and uncontrolled fires and extraction of adult plants from the wild for the horticulture trade have been shown to critically endanger populations of other saxicolous, narrow-range, endemics bromeliads in Brazil (Versieux and Wanderley 2007). Mineral extraction may also be a threat to this species. Filho and Tabarelli (2006) report that 67% of 86 species of Bromeliads studied in the Brazilian Atlantic Forest had less than six subpopulations. Twenty-five percent had only one known subpopulation. Forty-one subpopulations reported between 1920 and 1996 could not be relocated, 20 subpopulations had become extinct (Filho and Tabarelli 2006). Although some Dickia spp. have been reported to resist fires, with thick mantles of persisting insulating leaf bases (Benzing 2000), Versieux and Wendt (2007), during their field work in Minas Gerais, retuned to many places, where fires were so intense that entire populations disappeared within a few years, regardless of their saxicolous life forms. Fires may cause complete destruction of soil islands on rock outcrops preventing recolonization by the native flora and favouring the spread of invasive African grasses (Benzing 2000, Versieux and Wendt 2007).

Cryptanthus are popular houseplants and may be endangered by trade (Read 1989). Over the last 20 years, Bromeliads have become more popular in Brazil, as home and garden ornamentals and this has promoted increasing collection pressures on natural populations (Versieux and Wendt 2007). International trade of Bromeliaceae has also rapidly expanded (Read 1989). The long life cycle of xerophytic bromeliads increase the incentive to collect wild specimens for direct sale. Plants highly adapted to these hostile environments are appreciated for ornamental purposes. This creates an illegal commerce of plants extracted by local people and sold along the roads of the region (Versieux and Wendt 2007). In the northeastern Atlantic forest, some ornamental bromeliads have been intensively collected and almost eliminated from forest fragments close to human settlements (Filho and Tabarelli 2006).

Climate change may have a negative impact on the population of this species, through increased seedling mortality due to increased intensity of droughts and increased frequency and intensity of fires.

Conservation Actions [top]

Conservation Actions: This species was collected within the Chapada Diamantina National Park. In 1998, Leme (1999) reported that Bromeliaceae species found in this park could not be thought as protected because of the extensive habitat destruction taking place within its borders (Leme 1999, Pirrani 1999). It is unknown whether protection of rare plant species is now adequate within the park. Current occurrence of the taxon needs first to be confirmed. Field surveys must be conducted in the locality of the type collection and in suitable surrounding areas to define the range and the population size and whether extinction has occurred. Forty-one percent of the Caatinga has never been surveyed by scientists and 80% of what has been was surveyed poorly (Leal et al. 2005) although these figures may not hold true for the Chapada Diamantina. If found, conservation of the natural habitat and population of this species appears to be a priority. Filho and Tabarelli (2006) report that bromeliad species in the northeast Brazilian Atlantic forest are on the brink of extinction and are in urgent need of conservation . The taxon does not figure on the Brazilian list of threatened plants. Wild collections of C. diamantinensis, if they occur, should be banned. Breeding systems, patterns of genetic diversity and gene flow of the population and resilience to fire should be studied. C. diamantinensis is cultivated ex situ in botanical gardens. Seeds should be collected for germplasm conservation. Other species of Cryptanthus have been successfully micropropagated and the use of plants produced following this method for population reinforcement and for in vitro preservation programmes was suggested for other threatened Brazilian species (Arrabal et al. 2006). Sustainable ecotourism should be promoted.
