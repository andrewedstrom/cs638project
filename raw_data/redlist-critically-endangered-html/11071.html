Assessment Information [top]

Red List Category & Criteria: Critically Endangered B2ab(iii,v) ver 3.1
Year Published: 2015
Date Assessed: 2014-10-21
Assessor(s): Lubbe, A., Jordaan, M., Bragg, C., Paxton, B.R., Schumann, M., Van der Walt, R. & Ngobela, T.
Reviewer(s): Smith, K.
Contributor(s): Impson, D. & Swartz, E.R.
Justification:
Historically, Clanwilliam Sandfish (hereafter referred to as sandfish) were believed to have been a widespread species throughout the Olifants-Doring River system (Van Rensburg 1966) but quantitative data to support this is largely absent. Significant declines occurred in the main stream populations, likely as a result of the introduction of Black Bass Micropterus spp. in the 1930s. Quantitative surveys have been conducted post 2000 and while populations trends are better understood, the data may not be adequate to accurately evaluate population size reductions given the lack of comparable historical data. Given the absence of sandfish from the Olifants River, the low population numbers in the Doring River (and absence of juvenile and sub-adult individuals) and the high numbers of predatory invasive fish species in all tributaries where the species is known to occur, we believe a conservation status of Critically Endangered to be warranted under criterion B2ab(iii,v).  

Given the available data, an assessment of the area of occupancy (AOO) provided the most robust analysis of the sandfish population in line with the Guidelines for Using the IUCN Red List Categories and Criteria (IUCN Standards and Petitions Subcommittee 2013). We used extensive survey data over the last 14 years to determine the presence/absence of sandfish in the Doring and Olifants main streams and tributaries. All available data indicates that sandfish are now extinct from the Olifants main stream and tributaries. For the Doring main stream and tributaries, we used conservative estimates of both the available habitat length and width to calculate AOO (3.11 km2). Although we considered the full extent of potentially available habitat in the calculation of AOO, as discussed under the Distribution section below, only 19 km of river in the Oorlogskloof Nature Reserve can really be considered as viable habitat, encompassing both suitable spawning habitat and being absent of predatory alien fish species, thus allowing for recruitment. As a result, the sandfish population as a whole is severely fragmented (criteria B2a), with small adult populations confined to isolated upstream stretches in small tributaries where alien fish species are excluded by some natural barrier, and a main stream population which our available data indicates is becoming more heterogeneously distributed and scarce. In reality, there are strong grounds for arguing that the true sandfish AOO is confined to only the 19 km stretch of river in the Oorlogskloof Nature Reserve which has an area of 0.19 km2.

Our data also indicates that there is a continuing worrying decline of habitat quality and number of mature individuals (criteria B2biii,v), as evidenced by the recent invasion of the Oorlogskloof Nature Reserve by Banded Tilapia, and a more than 50% decline in catch per unit effort and presence of adult sandfish in the Doring main stream over the last decade.   

We believe there to be sufficient motivation for upgrading the sandfish’s conservation status from Endangered (Impson and Swartz, 2007) to Critically Endangered. The current assessment is based on more comprehensive data, particularly with regards to population trends in the Doring main stream, spawning behaviour and recruitment, which has made it apparent that the population is more fragmented than previously thought, with little to no recruitment taking place (this is the primary difference between this and the previous assessment). This information has made it clear that it is critically important to apply a more realistic species-specific habitat buffer to calculate AOO, as the viable population extent of the sandfish is in fact confined to only 19 km of river in the Oorlogskloof Nature Reserve, making it of the utmost importance to dedicate the required efforts to the conservation of this habitat and the species.

Geographic Range [top]

Range Description:The Clanwilliam Sandfish is endemic to the Olifants-Doring River system in the Western and Northern Cape provinces of South Africa (Skelton 2001). There is evidence that the species has been extirpated from the main stream of the Olifants River as, despite sampling efforts, no sandfish have been collected from the Olifants River since the mid-1980s (CapeNature research observation). The entirety of the sandfish population is therefore confined to the Doring River main stream (one of only 62 free flowing rivers in South Africa) and isolated tributaries of the Doring (see Figure 1 in the Supplementary Material); namely the 1. Oorlogskloof-Koebee, 2. Gif, 3. Kransgat, 4. Biedouw, 5. Tra-Tra, and 6. Matjies Rivers where sandfish have been recorded in the last five years (R. Van der Walt et al. unpublished). The Oorlogskloof-Koebee River in the Oorlogskloof Nature Reserve, a stretch of only 19 km of non-perennial river, provides habitat for the only known viably recruiting subpopulation of this species, where suitable spawning habitat exists and no predatory alien fish species are present. Due to very low numbers of adult sandfish and high predation by alien fish species, recruitment contributions from the remainder of the catchment are not expected to be significant.

Sandfish records in the Olifants River were recorded between 1960 and 1987, although no sandfish have been recorded in the Olifants since then (CapeNature Species Database). The current sandfish distribution is restricted to the Doring River and tributaries. Similar to the Olifants River, most of the Doring River system has been invaded by alien fish species, which makes the long term survival of the sandfish in this section of the system uncertain, as alien fish species are believed to be the primary cause of the extinction of sandfish in the Olifants River (Paxton et al. 2002). Currently adult sandfish are still present in the main stream Doring, where they co-exist with alien invasive fish species, and isolated tributaries where these alien fish species are excluded by some natural barrier such as a waterfall. The Tankwa, Dry Doring and Brandewyn rivers are indicated on the map (see Figure 1 in the Supplementary Material) as not being invaded by alien fish species. Unfortunately however, these rivers do not provide any habitat for indigenous species as they are completely dry for most of the year and lack the permanent deep pools which are required for sandfish.

To calculate the area of occupancy (AOO) for Clanwilliam Sandfish, we calculated the distance of river for all rivers in which sandfish has been recorded since 2000 (see Table 1 in the Supplementary Material). We included only the stretches of tributaries where sandfish occur which do not dry up during the summer, thus considered viable habitat. The distance of the Doring main stream River was calculated from the Matjies tributary confluence, as the Doring further upstream is known as the Dry Doring, completely drying out in the summer, down to the confluence with the Olifants River. For calculating AOO, only 50% of the total distance of the Doring main stream was included, as sandfish were caught at 28% and 42% of sampling sites on the Doring main stream in 2011 and 2013 respectively (see Figure 2 in the Supplementary Material), indicating that they are not present throughout the system. As per Impson and Swartz (2007) for the previous sandfish conservation assessment, we conservatively estimated the width of the active channel in summer (the dry season, therefore representative of the minimum available habitat) to be 10 m for tributaries and 30 m for the Doring main stream. These values were used as the buffer for calculating AOO. The calculation of AOO was done using the geoprocessing buffer tool in ArcGIS 10.1. Using these realistic habitat buffers, the total AOO for the sandfish is 3.11 km², compared to 3.5 km² reported by Impson and Swartz (2007) (see Table 1 in the Supplementary Material). The marginal difference in AOO between the two reports is due to the current calculation including more accurate information on the current distribution of sandfish, which has been collected by CapeNature in the last 5 years. We believe this to be an accurate and biologically meaningful reflection of the suitable habitat available to the sandfish.

The Guidelines for Using the IUCN Red List Categories and Criteria recommend calculating AOO using a reference grid size of 4 km² (IUCN Standards and Petitions Subcommittee 2013), in which case the sandfish AOO would be 517 km², using the method advised by IUCN (2013) for up-scaling AOO calculations. The definition for AOO stipulates that in some unique cases or contexts, the AOO should constitute “the smallest area essential at any stage to the survival of existing populations of a taxon”. Furthermore the guidelines specifically state that “the size of the area of occupancy will be a function of the scale at which it is measured, and should be at a scale appropriate to relevant biological aspects of the taxon, the nature of threats and the available data”. It is the opinion of the assessment team that the sandfish presents such a unique case, as the entire breeding population is confined to 18.66 km of river in the Oorlogskloof-Koebee River. Although adult fish are relatively widely distributed, this stretch of the Oorlogskloof-Koebee River is the only stretch of river throughout the sandfish’s distribution which is not invaded by predatory alien invasive fish species, and provides suitable spawning habitat, thus allowing for successful recruitment. The Oorlogskloof-Koebee therefore constitutes the smallest area essential at any stage to the survival of the sandfish. It is thus a critical site and highly essential to sandfish species persistence. Without it, the sandfish is on a path to extinction given the lack of other suitable spawning sites and the pervasive presence of predatory alien fish in the rest of its range. With reference to the scale appropriate to relevant biological aspects of the taxon, the Oorlogskloof-Koebee stretch must be considered as the width of the active channel of the river, which can be conservatively estimated at 10 m. Using the above reasoning, the true AOO for the sandfish is calculated at 0.19 km². 

 In the case of the sandfish we believe it to be appropriate to use a species-specific grid size, as the reference grid size significantly overestimates the AOO of the sandfish and is thus not biologically appropriate. It is important to consider that the rivers of the Western Cape are highly seasonal, linear habitats with active channels of well below the recommended reference grid size. The Oorlogskloof Nature Reserve is the only habitat which is both free of predatory alien species and provides suitable habitat for spawning and recruitment, and thus constitutes the only viable population.

Population [top]

Population:Historically, the main stream Doring River has been poorly sampled with very limited quantitative data collected prior to 2000. Extensive surveys were conducted in 2003 and again in 2011 and 2013 (Paxton et al. unpublished). Sampling was conducted at similar sites during the surveys using a large fyke net (Wing length: 20 x 2.5 m, Trap entrance: 2 x 1.5 m), at the end of winter, between the months of September to October. At each site, fyke nets were set between 17h00 and 18h00 in the evening and retrieved the following morning at 08h00.  

Sandfish catch data between main stream sites was variable for all surveys. The high variability in catches between sites is likely a result of the migratory nature of the sandfish and the relatively low population numbers. The variable nature of the data, as well as the lack of quantitative historical data makes it difficult to determine accurate population trends in the Doring River main stream. An effect of gear type on sampling success can be ruled out, as the large fyke has been shown to be the most effective non-lethal technique for sampling sandfish in the Doring main stream compared to small fyke, electrofishing and gill netting (B. Paxton, Freshwater Research Centre, pers. comm.). Any analysis of population trends is severely skewed by the extreme fluctuations in catch size, which are frequently either zero, or a large migrating school of fish, which makes any robust quantitative analysis between sampling sites over time and subsequent inferences on the overall population trend in the Doring River main stream difficult.  

However, foregoing comparative analyses between distinct sampling sites over time due to the above mentioned constraints, and evaluating each Doring main stream survey with multiple sampling sites as one sample, the sandfish population of the Doring main stream river as a whole can be characterized as relatively rare and heterogeneously distributed, with little to no recruitment taking place. This is supported by the following quantitative data from 2003, 2011, and 2013 Doring main stream surveys.

Figure 2 (see Supplementary Material) indicates that the Doring main stream system is dominated by predatory alien invasive fish species, predominantly Smallmouth Bass Micropterus dolomieu, Spotted Bass M. punctulatus and Bluegill Sunfish Lepomis macrochirus. The relative abundance of sandfish sampled decreased from 5.8% of all fish sampled in 2003 to 0.9% and 5.1% of the total catch in 2011 and 2013 respectively. In both 2011 and 2013, the number of sites at which sandfish were caught decreased by more than 50% compared to the 2003 survey. Similarly, the CPUE, with the effort standardized as one overnight fyke netting effort, decreased from 4.3 in 2003 to 0.6 and 2.3 fish per netting effort in 2011 and 2013 respectively. Combined this data illustrates the heterogeneous distribution of this population, which is likely influenced by aforementioned characteristics such as their migratory and schooling habits, and environmental conditions such as flow and food availability, which would influence their habitat selection and distribution. The ca. 50% decrease in presence/absence and CPUE over last decade in the Doring section of the system suggests that the sandfish population is likely decreasing and becoming more fragmented in the Doring river main stream. This is especially relevant given that there is strong evidence that they have been extirpated from the Olifants River main stream (CapeNature research observation).

 The available data suggests that the persistence of the current sandfish population may be due to the population now being predominantly comprised of old, large fish which are beyond the prey size class of the predatory alien invasive species. This is evident from the size class distribution of the different species sampled during the 2013 Doring main stream survey (Figure 3; see Supplementary Material). No indigenous fish species smaller than 400 mm (i.e. no juveniles or sub-adults) were recorded, indicating that there is no or minimal recruitment taking place.

In addition to the Doring main stream, sandfish presence has been reported in a number of Doring River tributaries, namely the Biedouw, Tra-Tra, Matjies, Kransgat, Oorlogskloof-Koebee and Gif Rivers (Figure 1; see Supplementary Material). These tributaries were surveyed in 2012, 2013 and 2014. All tributary populations are confined to very limited stretches of river where they are protected from alien invasive fish species by natural barriers such as small waterfalls. With the exception of the Oorlogskloof, all tributary populations consist of very low numbers of adult individuals (n< 10). In the Oorlogskloof river adult sandfish are common in a stretch of 19 km above a waterfall in the river which acts as an invasion barrier to black bass and bluegill sunfish. Adult sandfish are present below the barrier down to the Koebee river, but this population is unable to recruit successfully due to the presence of predatory alien fish species (R. Van der Walt. unpublished data). 

In 2011 the first record of sandfish spawning outside of the Oorlogskloof Nature Reserve was reported in the Biedouw River (R. Van der Walt pers. obs.). It is likely that sandfish use smaller tributaries such as the Biedouw to spawn, but unfortunately, these spawning events are unlikely to contribute significantly to population size. The reason for this is that the naturally seasonal nature of the tributaries (which is exacerbated by water abstraction for agriculture) are forcing the young of year fish into the Doring main stream in the dry summer months, where they are heavily predated by the alien fish population (as supported by the size class distribution data presented in Figure 3; see Supplementary Material). The Oorlogskloof Nature Reserve is therefore to the best of our knowledge home to the last remaining annually recruiting subpopulation of sandfish, and it is likely that this is the primary source of adult sandfish for the Doring main stream river, with only a minimal contribution to population growth from spawning in tributaries.  The Oorlogskloof Nature Reserve sandfish subpopulation is critical in terms of the survival of this highly threatened species, as it is the only viably recruiting subpopulation remaining. This makes the species as a whole extremely vulnerable to extinction. 

When the previous IUCN conservation status assessment was completed in 2007, the Oorlogskloof fish assemblage was pristine, with no alien species present. Subsequent to that assessment, in 2008, there was a large flood in the area which resulted in the nearby municipal dam overflowing and enabling the invasion of alien Banded Tilapia Tilapia sparrmanii into Oorlogskloof Nature Reserve system. The Banded Tilapia have since established themselves and are widespread in the system. Surveys were conducted in the Oorlogskloof River in 2000, 2010, 2013 and 2014 and population numbers of sandfish appear to be stable. Currently the Banded Tilapia appear to be having a limited impact on the sandfish population, as they are not aggressive predators. Banded Tilapia are, however, strong competitors for the same food resources as sandfish and are no doubt inflicting an additional competitive pressure on sandfish and other indigenous fish species. This incident only serves to highlight how vulnerable this already fragile population is to unpredictable changes to the system. The Oorlogskloof Nature Reserve management authority has since become aware of two additional farm dams bordering the Oorlogskloof Nature Reserve which are stocked with the alien invasive Mirror Carp Cyprinus carpio, and they are working to ensure that these fish are removed to reduce invasion risk into the Oorlogskloof. 

Beyond the Oorlogskloof Nature Reserve, the rest of the tributary populations are isolated and essentially “boxed in” by predatory invasive alien species. It is considered highly unlikely that these populations make any meaningful contribution to overall population size. The lack of suitable spawning habitat, recruitment, mortality, low densities, ageing population and heterogeneous distribution of the sandfish population in its last remaining habitat (the Doring River system) make this species extremely vulnerable to any extreme events or disease. Current data indicate that at best the population is stable. However, considering all the available information, particularly the fact that the main stream population is an ageing one, with little or no recruitment, and the only recruiting and previously pristine population has now also been invaded by an alien species, the precautionary assumption is that the population is decreasing and that the speed of the decline will increase as the mature fish are lost from the system and not replaced. 

The sandfish therefore qualifies for criterion B2a (severely fragmented, with little to no contribution from tributary populations to population growth) and B2b (continuing decline in habitat quality, as vindicated by the recent invasion of the Oorlogskloof by Banded Tilapia, and continuing decline in adult population numbers).

Habitat and Ecology [top]

Habitat and Ecology:Although little is known about the Clanwilliam Sandfish, they are more than likely rheophilic, requiring fast-flowing waters for spawning and deep pools for feeding, over-wintering and over-summering (Paxton et al. 2002). Indications are that sandfish are total spawners, i.e. they release one batch of eggs per year when conditions are favourable.  They are benthic detritivores, using sub-terminal mouths to feed on rocks.  Water quality from the tributaries that rise on the eastern flanks of the Cederberg and that flow into the Doring via the Groot, Tra-tra and Biedouw Rivers is influenced by the quartzitic sandstones of the Table Mountain and Witteberg Groups. These tend to be clear, low conductivity waters. During summer, convectional activity over the Karoo occasionally sends pulses of turbid, saline waters that drain the highly erodable shales and mudstones of the Dwyka Formation and Ecca Group into the Doring River. Waters flowing from these formations exhibit elevated levels of nutrients, conductivity and pH. Sandfish tend to be more common in these middle and northern reaches of the Doring River (highest abundances have been recorded from the Bos-Doring River confluence) suggesting that they derive some benefit from the Karoo-fed hydrologic and sediment regimes (Paxton et al. 2002).

The biology of this species is poorly studied. It co-exists with Labeobarbus capensis and Barbus serra in the larger rivers. It is a bottom feeder, feeding on algae, detritus and small invertebrates and it spawns in spring and summer (Skelton 2001).

Use and Trade [top]

Use and Trade: The species is not traded and only occasionally targeted by anglers. However, harvesting by indigenous peoples for food appears to be more common than initially suspected, particularly when they migrate up tributaries to spawn. Sandfish are particularly visible when they spawn as they congregate in shallow riffles and we have received anecdotal reports of sandfish being harvested in the Biedouw tributary, which is likely to put further pressure on this species.

Threats [top]

Major Threat(s): As with many of the other indigenous fish species in the Doring River, the primary threat to the persistence of the sandfish is predation and competition by alien invasive fish species, notably the centrarchids (Smallmouth Bass, Largemouth Bass and Bluegill Sunfish) which were introduced as sport fish by the then Department of Inland Fisheries during the earlier half of the twentieth century (de Moor and Bruton, 1988, Paxton et al. 2002). While the centrarchids present a considerable threat to the endemic species through predation and have completely replaced them where they occur, others such as Banded Tilapia pose a significant risk through competition and predation upon the young. To date, these alien species have resulted in the extinction of sandfish in approximately 80% of its historical distribution, and undoubtedly, alien invasive fish species introductions present the greatest future threat to the sandfish as in most instances invasions are near impossible or prohibitively expensive to reverse. An additional and potentially greater threat is the recent confirmed reports that Sharptooth Catfish (Clarias gariepinus) are present in the Olifants-Doring system. This species has been introduced, illegally in most cases, into all four primary river systems of the Western Cape (Jordaan et al. 2012). The impact of Sharptooth Catfish has not yet been quantified in the Western Cape but there is evidence to suggest that their impact will be severe (Cambray 2003, Clark et al. 2009, Breede River unpublished River Health Programme Survey 2009).  Their invasive success and associated impacts on indigenous fish fauna can be attributed to a range of factors, including their ability to survive in and adapt to a range of environmental conditions, their ability to survive desiccation, their omnivorous feeding habits, high fecundity and fast growth rate.

Much of the thinking that has focused attention on the importance of the natural flow regime for river fish populations in South Africa and elsewhere in the world, has been centred around the contribution that river flow makes to fish recruitment (e.g. Cambray et al. 1997, King et al. 1998). Although this process is not well understood in the case of the sandfish, there is substantive evidence, both anecdotal and from the known biology and ecology of closely related species, to support the contention that the species is a synchronous rheophilic spawner requiring optimal flow and temperature conditions for successful reproduction. Natural hydrological variability, together with water regulation and abstraction is therefore likely to play a major role in year-class strength. Water resources in the Doring River catchment are heavily exploited for irrigation agriculture, particularly during the dry summer months. The resulting no flow periods, especially in the more seasonal tributaries of the Doring River, confer an ecological advantage to the invasive fish species which have largely replaced the indigenous fish in these reaches (Paxton et al. 2002). 

It is considered highly probable that further water resources development on the Doring River will negatively impact on sandfish populations persisting in the main stream. In addition to major water resource developments and utilization, localized impacts are evident from farming activities (livestock and agricultural return flows) and invasion of riparian zones by invasive alien tree species which alters riparian habitats and reduces run off. Reduced water quality due to livestock grazing—particularly goats in the Biedouw Valley area—within the riparian zone may contribute to mortality of tributary populations over the summer when fish are concentrated in isolated pools.

Conservation Actions [top]

Conservation Actions: The largest recruiting population of sandfish is in the Oorlogskloof River system. A large part of the river where the sandfish occurs is located within the Oorlogskloof Provincial Nature Reserve in the Northern Cape Province.  An active annual monitoring programme of the river has been initiated in 2010.  In order to formalize conservation actions for this species in the rest of its distribution range, a biodiversity management plan (BMP) was drafted in 2012 which identified a list of potential conservations actions, along with potential implementing agents and timelines (Paxton et al. 2012).  This management plan is in the process of being signed off for implementation by the national Department of Environmental Affairs.

Currently, a partnership project between the Endangered Wildlife Trust (South African NGO) and Government nature conservation departments; CapeNature and Northern Cape Department of Environment and Nature Conservation, are working towards implementing the conservation actions outlined in the sandfish BMP, with the following projects currently underway:

1. High risk dams for the potential further introduction of alien species to the Oorlogskloof have been identified and we are working towards clearing these dams of alien species.

2. We have implemented an “on-the-ground” awareness campaign with local communities and farmers surrounding critical sandfish habitats, as well as a media campaign targeting popular wildlife and conservation publications to raise awareness of the uniqueness of our indigenous fish biota and the threats they face.

3. We are working with farmers to improve on-farm irrigation efficiency, which will allow for more water to sustain ecosystem function and important fish habitats.

4. A conservation action initiated in 2014 is the rehabilitation of the Biedouw River with the long term action of removing alien invasive fishes and restoring environmental flows in a large section of the river to ensure increased survival of young sandfish.  Once rehabilitated, the Biedouw River will be the focus of a conservation translocation to supplement the low numbers of adult sandfish currently in the river.  A rehabilitation plan is in the process of being compiled and a short term conservation action that has been identified is the removal of young of year sandfish from downstream areas where they will either die from desiccation or predation towards the end of summer and returning these fish to their upstream spawning areas where they are likely to survive.  As these fish will be larger and stronger by the time they attempt a migration to the main Doring in the following year, it is expected that their chances of survival to adulthood will be improved. This process will be guided by IUCN best management practices for conservation translocations.
