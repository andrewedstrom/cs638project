Kingdom Phylum Class Order Family

Scientific Name: Thorectes variolipennis
Species Authority: Marseul, 1876
Synonym(s):
Thorectes subcostatus Fairmaire, 1883
Taxonomic Source(s): Schoolmeesters, P. 2015. Scarabs: World Scarabaeidae Database (version Jan 2015). Available at: www.catalogueoflife.org/col.

Assessment Information [top]

Red List Category & Criteria: Endangered B2ab(iii,iv) ver 3.1
Year Published: 2015
Date Assessed: 2014-07-23
Assessor(s): Ruiz, J.L., Verdú, J.R., Lumaret, J.P. & Labidi, I.
Reviewer(s): Gerlach, J. & Numa, C.
Justification:

It's a rare and little known species distributed in a small area of occupancy (AOO of 32 km2). It is distributed in two subpopulations, one in inner sandy soils which is not confirmed and is separated 200 km from the main sub population in the Atlantic coast. There is a continuing decline in habitat quality and area due to urbanisation, agriculture intensification and even pollution by pesticides in the more traditional crops. It is known from seven localities, its main habitat in sandy soils of coastal areas is also a potential area for urban expansion and agriculture intensification. Therefore, the number of locations is between seven to nine but never exceeding ten locations. The population is considered severely fragmented.

This species is precautionarily listed as Endangered B2ab(iii,iv). It is necessary to investigate the presence of a stable subpopulation in Kasba Tadla and also the persistence and population trend of this species in the recorded localities from coastal Morocco.

Geographic Range [top]

Range Description:This species is endemic to southwest Morocco, mainly in Atlantic coast (Baraud 1965, 1985, Löbl et al. 2006). It has been recorded from nine localities which correspond to two subpopulations: the coastal subpopulation, with eight localities from Casablanca to Safi, north to south (e.g. following localities: Safi, El Bebdouza (= Cap Cantin), Oualidia, El Jadida, Azemmour, Casablanca), and the inland Tadla subpopulation (middle Atlantic plains: Tadla plateau), with a single locality, Kasba Tadla  (Marseul 1876, Martínez de la Escalera 1914, Kocher 1953, 1958, Baraud, 1965, 1985, Boucher 1990, Tauzin 1990, Miessen 2002, Facheaux et al. 2006, Lobo et al. 2010).  It exists at a disjunct, peripheral and isolated subpopulation at Kasba Tadla region. It is quite possible that the species penetrate inland through the river valleys that cross the Moroccan Atlanticplains (e.g. Oum er Rbia river).

The species was described from “Dahr-el-Beida” (=Casablanca, Morocco) by Marseul (1876); also, this author made the following comment (sic) “Cette espèce, decouverte aux environs de Mogador par M. Trovey-Blackemore (…)”. Since then, the species has never been found in Mogador, currently Essaouira (e.g. M. Martínez de la Escalera sampled for years at that location and did not find it); thus, this record is in all probability wrong. The synonym Geotrupes subcostatus was described from “Maroc”, without specifying locality (Fairmaire 1883).The extent of occurrence (EOO) is around 21,576 km2 and the area of occupancy (AOO) is estimated at 32 km2. The altitude ranges from zero to 550 m asl.

Population [top]

Population:No data exists on the population size or trends. Boucher (1990) suggested that the species would be relatively abundant between Safi and Oualidia. Field observations in 1992 from Oualidia (J.L. Ruiz pers. obs.) showed that this species was relatively frequent in the coastal plain (grasslands). Considering that this species could have a biology and ecology close to other coastal Thorectes species (such as T. distinctus), it is possible that this species could be abundant locally but with isolated subpopulations. The population is considered severely fragmented.
Current Population Trend:Unknown

Habitat and Ecology [top]

Habitat and Ecology:Thorectes variolipennis is a stenoic species. The majority of the localities where the species has been recorded correspond to open areas in coastal zones of low altitude, at thermomediterranean bioclimatic level,  mainly in the following habitat types: coastline dunes with psammophilous vegetation (e.g. Ammophila arenaria, Retama monosperma, Juniperus phoenicea), fixed sands and subcoastal plains (substrate of carbonate rocks, Doukkala plain) with grasslands and scrub steppes (e.g. Pistacia lentiscus, Chamaerops humilis Ziziphus lotus, Olea europaea var. sylvestris), and also fallow farm fields. It has been found both on sandy and rocky soils (Boucher 1990, J.L. Ruiz pers. obs. 1992). Based on satellite images, much of the distribution range of the species is occupied by crops, especially in the plains near the coastline and adjacent lands (Chaouïa and Doukkala plains); so, in these areas the natural habitats have been lost or are seriously altered. The species is coprophagous and mainly eats sheep and goat droppings, but also horse and cow dung; it excavates shallow burrows (approximate depth of 15 cm) (Boucher 1990, Facheaux et al. 2006, J.L. Ruiz pers. obs. 1992). Adults are active in autumn (october) and spring (march to may) (Boucher 1990, Miessen 2002, Facheaux et al. 2006, J.L. Ruiz pers. obs. 1992).
Systems:Terrestrial
Continuing decline in area, extent and/or quality of habitat:Yes

Use and Trade [top]

Use and Trade: This species is not utilised.

Threats [top]

Major Threat(s):

The main threats affecting the species are related to the degradation and loss and loss of suitable habitat, due to the increase of urbanization in littoral areas of Morocco (linked to tourism) and, especially, excessive development of the intensive agriculture, with indiscriminate use of pesticides. According to satellite images from Google Earth, the littoral and sub coastal lands near Casablanca and El Jadida-Azemmour and, by extension, the middle Atlantic plains (mainly Doukkala and Chaouïa plains), has been highly altered and this is likely to continue, thus suggesting further continuing decline in the extent and quality of habitat. Otherwise, the current presence of a well established sub population of the species in the peripheral locality of Kasba Tadla (Tadla plateau ) must be cheked, because this record is 50 years ago (Baraud 1965).

As occurs in other dung beetles species, changes in land uses (e.g. schrublands to crops, intensive agriculture) or in agro pastoral practices (livestock abandonment, livestock intensification and some veterinary treatments) which affect soil structure, dung availability and dung quality could influence on the number of individuals at local and landscape levels (e.g. Lumaret et al. 1993, 2012, Hutton and Giller 2003, Lobo et al. 2006).

Conservation Actions [top]

Conservation Actions: There are no known species-specific conservation measures in place for this species. It is not recorded from any protected areas. Main conservation actions should be directed to the protection of coastal areas where this species exists. It should be minimized the intensive agricultural (with indiscriminate and massive use of pesticides), urban development (mainly linked to tourism) and associated infrastructure in the Atlantic coastline and adjacent lands of central and southern Morocco, to preserve the preferential habitat of the species. More research is needed to know the distribution, ecology and threats; especially, it is necessary to discern the actual presence of a stable sub population in the Kasba Tadla region (and the possible presence of intermediate populations between this and the Atlantic coast), and also the population trends of the species in the recorded localities from Morocco.
