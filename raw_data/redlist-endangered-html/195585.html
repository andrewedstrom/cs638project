Taxonomy [top]

Kingdom Phylum Class Order Family
Animalia Mollusca Gastropoda Stylommatophora Orthalicidae

Scientific Name: Placostylus seemanni
Species Authority: (Dohrn, 1861)
Common Name(s):
English – Flax Snail
Synonym(s):
Bulimus seemanni Dohrn, 1861
Taxonomic Notes: Placostylus seemanni has in the past been considered to consist of two subspecies, Placostylus seemanni seemanni and Placostylus seemanni mbengensis. In this assessment we follow the treatment of Barker et al. (2005) and consider these taxa as full species, i.e., Placostylus seemanni  and Placostylus mbengensis.

Barker et al. (2005) consider that species status should be afforded based on differences in niche (arboreal vs ground-dwelling), habitat (lowland vs montane), and conchological features noted by Cooke (1942).

Assessment Information [top]

Red List Category & Criteria: Endangered B1ab(iii)+2ab(iii) ver 3.1
Year Published: 2012
Date Assessed: 2011-08-25
Assessor(s): Barker, G. & Brodie, G.
Reviewer(s): Cowie, R., Triantis, K., García, N. & Pippard, H.
Justification:
This species has a very restricted distribution. It is known from a maximum of three locations on the island of Kadavu in Fiji, where its total extent of occurrence (EOO) and its area of occupancy (AOO) are less than 308 km2. Its forest habitat is declining in quality due to introduced invasive species (rats and pigs) and to a slower extent, due to the expansion of human settlement particularly for agriculture. These threats are likely to continue as human populations increase. The risk for introduction of the predatory mongoose is high and there is no legal protection in place for the habitats required by this land snail. The species is therefore listed as Endangered. Continued biosecurity vigilance is critical to prevent further invasive species establishments in this species' habitat. Surveys to determine the current distribution and population size, as well as monitoring of the species' habitat trends are recommended.

Geographic Range [top]

Range Description:

This species is restricted to the island of Kadavu (408 km2), approximately 100 km off the south coast of Viti Levu, Fiji. Collection records with locational data are all from the western end of the island around the highest mountain (Nabukelevu) at 805 metres.

Under the assumption that this species is restricted to forest, the extent of occurrence (EOO) and area of occupancy (AOO) are inferred to be less than 308 km2 based on the known forest cover of Kadavu in 1990-1993 (Fiji National Forest Inventory). Based on threats from known invasive species, the number of locations is estimated as three, as the island’s forest habitat can be geographically separated into three sections joined only by relatively narrow (< 4 km) “land bridges” (i.e. the forested areas on each of the three subsections are not strongly connected to each other, particularly between the western and central sections where human development is pronounced).

Population [top]

Population:

No mention of abundance was made in the original description of this species (Dohrn 1861). However up to 42 specimens were collected by Mann in 1916 (Barker et al. 2005, Barker unpublished data). Garrett (1872) listed the species as abundant and peculiar to Kandavu [sic]. The species has been collected in lesser numbers (< 10) on five occasions with locality data since 1916. The most recent record for this species is the collection of five specimens by the Wildlife Conservation Society in 2006 (Barker and Bouchet 2010). There have been no recent surveys on Kadavu. Accordingly, the current population size is unknown.

Current Population Trend:Unknown

Habitat and Ecology [top]

Habitat and Ecology:

This species is reported by Barker et al. (2005) to be terrestrial, i.e. found at ground level, in lowland to high elevation forests. However, Parkinson (1990) stated that collectors from the Goddefroy Museum in Hamburg reported observing the species seven metres up trees near the summit of Mt Washington (Nabukelevu).  Little else about its ecology is known. However, based on the study of Placostylus species in other Oceanian countries (Brescia et al. 2008, Parish et al. 1995, Stringer et al. 2004) members of the genus have very limited dispersal ability (Ponder et al. 2003) and are likely to be closely linked to forest areas as they have relatively specific microhabitat requirements such as calcium rich soils (Brescia 2001) and well-shaded moist, leaf litter and broad-leaf forest plant species with relatively smooth trunks. Based on inference from other members of the genus in the region (Parrish et al. 1995, Stringer et al. 2004, Brescia 2008) individual life span is likely to be between 3-20 years. 

Systems:Terrestrial
Continuing decline in area, extent and/or quality of habitat:Yes

Use and Trade [top]

Use and Trade: This species is sought after by collectors.

Threats [top]

Major Threat(s):

Approximately half of Fiji’s original forests have been lost through clearance for agriculture and via repeated fires, and large areas of remaining forest have been heavily degraded (Masibalavu and Dutson 2006). The 1990-93 Fiji national forestry inventory estimated the total land area under dense- and medium dense-forest cover on Kadavu (including mahogany and pine plantations) to be 225 km2. An updated forest inventory is expected to be released soon based on 2007 data. None of the forest has any form of legal protection although the area surrounding Mount Nabukelevu is well recognized by various conservation groups as a priority forest location because it is the only area in west Kadavu that retains extensive “old-growth” forest.  The area is under traditional ownership by three different local villages each located on a different side of the mountain.

Kadavu has introduced cats, rats and Yellow Crazy Ants but is currently free from the notorious introduced predatory mongoose, which is very common and a severe threat to endemic animal species on neighboring islands within the Fiji group (Masibalavu and Dutson 2006). Rats and pigs are a known threat to Placostylus species in other Oceania countries (Sherley et al. 1998, Parrish et al. 1995). The presence of this species or any other endemic snails is not currently recognized or acknowledged by the local indigenous community. In Fiji land snails are generally viewed by almost all communities as either valueless or crop pests.

Conservation Actions [top]

Conservation Actions:

BirdLife International has designated two priority bird conservation areas (IBAs) on Kadavu: Nabukelevu (= Mount Washington) in the far west (IBA area 29 km2) and East Kadavu (IBA 78 km2). An isolated ecotourism resort at Matava on the southeastern side of Kadavu is beginning to develop conservation programmes with the local NGO NatureFiji/MareqetiViti.

Surveys to assess population size and distribution are needed, and local expertise should be developed to assist with such surveys. Determining the extent of invasive species threats is critical. Establishing any linkages between these snails and community land owners is also needed. Furthermore, assessing the genetic relatedness to other endemic Placostylus species in Fiji and the wider western Pacific region is recommended.

There may be opportunities for surveys and awareness piggybacking on future conservation projects planned for Kadavu by BirdLife International and the local NGO NatureFiji/MareqetiViti.
