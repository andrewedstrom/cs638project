Taxonomy [top]

Kingdom Phylum Class Order Family
Plantae Tracheophyta Liliopsida Bromeliales Bromeliaceae

Scientific Name: Tillandsia pseudomontana
Species Authority: W.Weber & Ehlers
Taxonomic Notes: Generic limits and relationships in Tillandsioideae are currently undergoing changes (Barfuss et al. 2005).

Assessment Information [top]

Red List Category & Criteria: Endangered B1ab(iii) ver 3.1
Year Published: 2014
Date Assessed: 2013-10-25
Assessor(s): Romand-Monnier, F. & Lutz, M.
Reviewer(s): Messina, T. & Santos Filho, L.
Contributor(s): Moraes, M.
Justification:
Tillandsia pseudomontana is a little known, recently described, small atmospheric bromeliad, which is only known from the type collection made in 1981, in Sao Bento, Santa Catarina, southern Brazil (Weber 1983, Roguenant 2001). The municipality of Sao Bento comprises an area of 495 km2, of which approximately 30% (ca 150 km2) comprise original vegetation. The extent of occurrence (EOO) is estimated to be between 150 km2 and 500 km2. The taxon was collected in the Arauacaria Moist forests ecoregion, within the Atlantic Forest Biome. Most of the native forest cover in the region has been lost and only isolated fragments or degraded, species-poor forest regrowth remain. Tree dependent epiphytes, such as T. pseudomontana, are threatened by habitat loss, degradation, fragmentation, biological simplification and collapse of ecological processes of the forest (Filho and Leme 2007, Toledo-Aceves and Wolf 2008). Newly described, seriously threatened plant species are constantly discovered. The fact that many bromeliad species are known from only one subpopulation is not due to uneven sampling effort but because they represent the last remnants of an earlier biodiversity (Filho and Leme 2007). Many populations of bromeliads in this region have been depleted as a result of uncontrolled harvesting for ornamental and ceremonial purposes. Conservation efforts have so far been extremely limited. The taxon was not collected within a conservation unit nor does it benefit from legal protection in Brazil. Based on present knowledge, given the very narrow geographic distribution, the high habitat specificity, the high level of threats to epiphytic bromeliads in the region (such as habitat loss and degradation) and the lack of protection of the taxon, T. pseudomontana is rated as Endangered.

Habitat and Ecology [top]

Habitat and Ecology:The taxon was collected at ca 900 m asl within the Araucaria moist forests ecoregion (specimen data, GIS data), which are part of the Atlantic forest of southern Brazil. Araucaria moist forests are cloud forests of mixed coniferous and broad-leafed trees, which spread out across a mountainous landscape (da Silva 2001). Seeds have a plumose coma and are wind-dispersed, however, a high proportion of seeds in epiphytic Tillandsia are not dispersed and dispersed seeds rarely reach a greater distance than the crown of their host. Dispersal by wind leads to spatial aggregation on a local scale (Mondragon and Calvo-Irabien 2006, Cascante-Marin et al. 2008). Germination in situ is low in Tillandsia species and seedling mortality is high, mainly due to drought and falling of individuals as a result of branch fall and dislodging (Mondragon and Calvo-Irabien 2006,Toledo-Aceves and Wolf 2008). The survival rate of juvenile Tilllandsia spp. increases with canopy openness (Hietz-Seifert et al. 1996). This species is a small epiphytic bromeliad that resembles T. montana and T. seidelium but differs by its connate, high sepals (Weber 1983, Roguenant 2001).
Systems:Terrestrial
Continuing decline in area, extent and/or quality of habitat:Yes

Threats [top]

Major Threat(s): Epiphytes have experienced dramatic population decreases, in the neotropics, mainly because of habitat loss and extraction activities, which have driven many species close to extinction (Mondragon and Calvo-Irabien 2006). The Araucaria forest is a highly threatened subtropical ecosystem. An estimated 90% of the native forest cover has been lost (da Silva 2001, Zanelli et al. 2005). Illegal timber extraction, firewood collection, forest conversion into agricultural lands, fires, extractivism, poverty, indifference of settlers, climate change, introduction of exotic species, pollution, demographic explosion, hunting which eliminate vertebrate seed dispersers, selective logging which commonly favour large trees and urban expansion all represent threats to the future of these forests (da Silva 2001, Filho and Leme 2007). Forests in mountainous areas where steep slopes and remoteness of terrain makes timber extraction difficult are more likely to be preserved (Baptipsta and Rudel 2006, Filho and Leme 2006).

Fragmentation of forests is a major cause of bromeliad extinction. It causes local and regional extirpation of emergent trees (key habitats for epiphytic bromeliads) and vertebrate seed dispersers, a reduction in pollinator abundance and leads to habitat desiccation and invasion of ruderal plants. Fragments are therefore more prone to fire, which appears to be detrimental to many bromeliad species (Benzing 2000, Ariani et al. 2004, Filho and Leme 2006, Hietzt-Seifert et al. 2006). Forest fragmentation also facilitates the extraction of ornamental species (Benzing 2000, Filho and Tabarelli 2006), including many Tillandsia spp. However, microclimatic conditions in fragmented landscapes, which are generally drier and more exposed force a shift from shade loving, mesic to sun loving and drought tolerant bromeliads (Wolf 2005, Filho and Leme 2006, Cascante-Marin et al. 2009). Type V Tillandsia like T. pseudomontana, are usually drought tolerant and often heliophile. Atmospheric (type V) Tillandsia, are frequently reported to occur in higher abundance compared to other bromeliad species and types of epiphytes in disturbed sites, where higher light and drought stress are common (Cascante-Marin et al. 2008, Reyes-Garcia et al. 2008).

The taxon does not appear to be available for sale. Tillandsia species are relatively difficult to propagate and take several years to flower from seeds. Propagation by offsets, which form after flowering, is only in a limited number. These increase the incentive to collect wild specimen for direct sale. There is considerable evidence that many plants for sale are collected from the wild in Central and South America. In the Northeastern Atlantic Forest, some ornamental bromeliads have been intensively collected and almost eliminated from forest fragments close to human settlements (Filho and Tabarelli 2006). Bromeliads with narrow geographic distributions, such as T. pseudomontana can be considered extremely vulnerable to commercial exploitation (Read 1989).

Tropical montane forests are especially sensitive to atmospheric climate change and likely to be the most affected. Predicted increase in dry seasons, drought and reduction in cloud formation patterns are expected to cause the replacement of many of the narrow altitude range cloud forests by lower altitude ecosystems, as well as the expulsion of peak residing cloud forests into extinction. Epiphytes with very high habitat specificity are unlikely to colonize these modified ecosystems, which may favour the invasion of exotic trees and pathogens. Migration will be limited by habitat fragmentation. Increased hurricanes and intense rainstorms may also damage cloud forest (Winkler et al. 2007, Zotzt and Bader 2009).

Conservation Actions [top]

Conservation Actions: This species was not collected within any conservation unit. Less than 1% of the ecoregion is covered by protected areas (da Silva 2001). In the Atlantic forest, Bromeliad species with less than six known subpopulations will only survive if the fragments containing the last populations are declared as protected areas (Filho and Tabarelli 2006). The Serra do Mar Corridor, which may include the type locality, is currently being implemented as part of the PP-G7 Atlantic forest subprogramme. Extensive habitat restoration and expansion of protected areas is needed for the establishment of corridors (Filho and Leme 2007). Many forest fragments occur on private land. The establishment of a large and well designated network of private reserves and the creation of innovative incentive are priorities. Law enforcement need to be improved and especially the Forest Code (Tabarelli et al. 2005). The taxon does not appear to be grown ex situ and does not benefit from any legal protection in Brazil. Current occurrence of the taxon needs first to be confirmed. Surveys and census should be conducted in the type locality and in potential sites of occurrence to determine the current size and range of the population and whether local extinction has occurred. The population should be closely monitored and its dynamics studied. The habitat conditions, availability of propagules, dispersal characteristics and requirement for seedlings establishment as well as breeding system and genetic structure of the population should be studied (Cascante-Marín et al. 2008). The level of habitat specificity and ecological requirements need to be characterized. Research should be conducted on the impact of forest fragmentation, extraction, climate change on the population and on the ability of the taxon to colonise disturbed sites. Extraction of the taxon in the wild should be banned as removal may represent a great loss of genetic diversity. Seeds should be collected for the purpose of ex situ and germplasm conservation. Micropropagation protocols should be designed for population reinforcement and for in vitro preservation (Arrabal et al.2002). Trees can be manually repopulated with epiphyte seedlings, which offer the possibility to restore genetic variation (Toledo-Aceve and Wolf 2008).
