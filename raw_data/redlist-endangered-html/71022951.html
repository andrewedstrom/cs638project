Taxonomy [top]

Kingdom Phylum Class Order Family
Plantae Tracheophyta Magnoliopsida Santalales Santalaceae

Scientific Name: Okoubaka aubrevillei
Species Authority: Pellegr. & Normand
Common Name(s):
English – Death Tree
Synonym(s):
Octoknema okoubaka Aubrév. & Pellegr
Taxonomic Notes:

First collected in Cote d’Ivoire in 1936, it was named Octoknema okoubaka Aubrév. & Pellegr and placed in the family Octoknemaceae (Aubrevillei and Pellegrin 1937). After further studies in the mid-20th century, it was renamed Okoubaka aubrevillei (Pelleg & Norman), and eventually placed in family Santalaceae (Normand 1944, Stauffer 1957, Halle 1987). Concluding from molecular phylogenetic studies, the taxon was suggested to be placed in the family Cervantesiaceae (Dar and Nickrent 2008).

Assessment Information [top]

Red List Category & Criteria: Endangered C2a(i) ver 3.1
Year Published: 2015
Date Assessed: 2015-03-04
Assessor(s): Borokini, T.I., Rivers, M.C. & Wheeler, L.
Reviewer(s): Cheek, M. & Gosline, G.
Justification:
Okoubaka aubrevillei meets the criteria for the Endangered category due to its small population size. The population size is estimated to 100-2,500 mature individuals. The mature individuals are scattered widely distant from each other and the subpopulations consist of fewer than 10 trees. Furthermore, the population was noted to have reduced by 25% in the last 50 years based on comparison of locations of herbarium records taken in the past 60 years and present conditions. Though it has a wide distribution range, it has been shown to be rare in all its reported localities, while high demand for its bark and seeds for medicine as well as its highly priced wood has influenced the decimation of the population in all its range. Therefore, Okoubaka aubrevillei has been assessed as Endangered C2a(i).

Geographic Range [top]

Range Description:Okoubaka aubrevillei is distributed and endemic to the Guinean West African rainforest, spanning from Republic of Guinea to Democratic Republic of Congo. The tree has been reported from 12 countries within this region: Guinea Republic, Sierra Leone, Liberia, Cote d’Ivoire, Burkina Faso, Ghana, Benin Republic, Nigeria, Cameroon, Central African Republic, Gabon and Democratic Republic of Congo (Borokini 2015). The tree is observed to be more abundant in Democratic Republic of Congo, Liberia and Sierra Leone than in the rest of the countries. However, the centre of diversity for the tree is in Democratic Republic of Congo where two sister taxa to this tree are located.

The presence of the tree is doubtful in Benin Republic, as the only two references to the tree in the country did not specifically mention sighting the tree. Herbarium records from relevant Beninoise institutions may be necessary to confirm its presence. The same is true of Burkina Faso, where the only referenced document reported its ethnobotanical use in the country.

Population [top]

Population:The tree is reported to be rare in all known localities for the tree. It was estimated that less than 20 stands remain in Nigeria (Osemeobo 2007). Generally, experts, medicinal plant gatherers and vendors in the countries where the tree is found describe its rarity. The population of the tree appears to have declined sharply in the last 60 years, as the tree is absent from many localities in Cameroon, Ghana, Sierra Leone and Côte d’Ivoire where herbarium samples were collected in the 20th century. As a result, over 25% of the population is estimated to have been lost in the last 50 years. Although the exact number of mature individuals for this tree is unknown, field work indicate <100 mature trees while the population size including coppices and saplings is about 250 individuals across the entire range. However, due to difficulty in counting every individual in a population that is so widespread, the population size (in terms of mature individuals) is estimated to be 100-2,500, with a best estimate of 250. Most matured trees are found to be solitary and distant from others, while most seeds dispersed beside the mother tree do not germinate. Very few cases of stands located in proximity to each other were reported from Ivory Coast, Ghana and Liberia. The number of matured individuals in a subpopulation is one to 10 (Borokini 2015). In most cases, the tree exists as solitary individual stands, some of which are saplings and coppices.

Habitat and Ecology [top]

Habitat and Ecology:Okoubaka aubrevillei is a tree species growing up to 40 metres in height. It is found in West African Guinean rainforest, preferring forest gaps or creating its own gap by its hemi-parasitic potentials. It has also been reported on rocky hills in the forest and along slopes (Borokini 2015). The major ecological significance of the tree is its hemi-parasitic potentials, developing root haustoria to kill other trees around it (Veenendaal et al. 1996). It is observed to be a non-pioneer light demander (NLPD) hence, it is believed that the hemi-parasitic potential was evolved to create gap for its saplings to have direct access to sunlight, a condition necessary for the survival and establishment of its saplings (Hawthorne 1995). Pollinators and dispersers for the tree are unknown due to research neglect and a paucity of scientific information on the tree. However, due to its large seed mass, Hawthorne (1995) suggested that African forest elephants may be responsible for its dispersal, while porcupines and chimpanzees were also reported to feed on its seeds and leaves (Hockings 2007, Ladipo et al. 2008). Photographs taken in Ghana by MD Swain in 1977 indicate that the tree also act as phorophyte for epiphytes.
Systems:Terrestrial
Generation Length (years):100

Use and Trade [top]

Use and Trade: The bark and seeds are harvested and traded locally in medicinal markets in all countries where it is found. The barks are also traded at the international market for the production of homeopathic drugs for human and veterinary use in Europe and North America (Borokini 2015). It is considered a sacred tree and is used for magical purposes in most of its locations in West Africa.

Threats [top]

Major Threat(s):

Okoubaka aubrevillei is observed to have a natural rare occurrence within its natural distribution range, which is a natural extinction risk for the tree. This risk is further accentuated by heavy debarking pressure for local medicinal use and international trade. The oldest published record of debarking was in Nigeria by Hardie (1963) in which he noted heavy debarking of a stand in Sapoba Forest Reserve in spite of traditionally-instituted prohibitions for harvesting the tree in the 1950s. Experts report that the tree is felled to allow for space for farming in Cote d’Ivoire. Selective logging for its wood may have occurred in the past decades, but it is no longer common in wood markets across the region.

It is considered a sacred tree in most parts of West Africa, but most traditional prohibitions surrounding the tree are no longer respected. This may be due to economic gains from the bark sales, politically-motivated weakening of traditional institutions in Africa, scientific enlightenment and proselytization to other religions (Borokini 2015). It is projected that international trade on the tree will increase, thus increasing bark harvesting pressure on the tree. Currently, herbarium curators, botanists, medicinal plant gatherers and vendors in most countries indicate that the tree is very scarce (van Andel et al. 2014).

Conservation Actions [top]

Conservation Actions:

The remaining subpopulations of the tree are restricted to protected areas and a few other locations where the tree is worshipped. Yet, many of the individuals in the protected areas are affected by heavy harvesting pressure, indicating that they are not really protected in the protected areas. Strict protection and management of protected areas have been characterized by widespread encroachment, poor staffing, inadequate funding, presence of enclave villages, land conversion to farming and several other illegal activities in Nigeria and other African countries (Borokini 2005). Burkill (1985) noted the Liberian Government ban on the sale of the tree barks decades ago, but it is unclear whether the ban was effective and still in effect today. Traditional beliefs are the main form of protection for this species, traditional institutions however are losing their significance and respect in many parts of West Africa. The fears for the taboos that had helped to protect some sacred trees are no longer relevant, while scientific research has explained and de-mystified some “mysterious phenomena” surrounding some sacred trees, including the hemi-parasitism potentials of Okoubaka. As a result of scientific enlightenment, relative economic hardship and demand for its wood, harvesters can easily approach the tree and harvest its parts without restriction in many places. It has not been recorded in any ex situ collections.
