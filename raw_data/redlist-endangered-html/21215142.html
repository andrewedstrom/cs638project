Justification:

This species is assessed as Endangered based on an inferred reduction of the population of 54% in the last ten years, and a strong and continuing decline in the population size in the last ten years as a result of known, ongoing and irreversible causes (A2) according to an index of abundance appropriate for the taxa (b), a reduction in the EOO, and as a result of the effects of introduced taxa (e) as several published reports suggest that pathogens introduced from non-native introduced bumblebees is an ongoing and increasing threat to this species.

Geographic Range [top]

Range Description:

Bombus dahlbomii is distributed in the southern tip of South America, which comprises two countries: Argentina and Chile, (Abrahamovich and Diaz 2001, Montalva et al. 2011).  In Argentina, this species has been recorded (i.e. at least one pinned specimen exists) in the following provinces (subcountry units, from North to South): Mendoza, Buenos Aires, Neuquén, Río Negro, Chubut and Santa Cruz (Abrahamovich and Diaz 2001). In Chile, it has been recorded in the following regions (subcountry units, from North to South): IV (Coquimbo), V (Valparaíso), Metropolitan Region, VI (OHiggins), VII (Maule), VIII (BíoBío), IX (La Araucanía,  including Mocha Island), X (Los Lagos , including Chiloé Island), XI (Aisén) and XII (Magellanes, including the Chilean portion of Tierra del Fuego Island), (Ruiz 1936, Toro and Chiapa 1997, Montalva et al. 2011, Tavie et al. accepted).

The northern limit of the species’ geographic range is located in the IV Chilean Region (Ruiz 1936). The NE limit is in the NW of Mendoza Province in Argentina (Abrahamovich and Diaz 2001). However, the occurrence in Mendoza is based on a single recorded queen (collection date unknown, deposited at the La Plata Museum Argentina, hereafter, MLP and published >15 years ago), and pollination ecologists and bee biologists recently consulted who worked intensively in the field during the last ten years failed to detect this species in Mendoza (N. Chacoff, G. Debandi, J.P. Torretta, D. Medan, and D.Vazquez pers. comm.).

The eastern limit is the Atlantic coast in the Argentinean Province of Buenos Aires (Abrahamovich and Diaz 2001). However, the presence in this province is based on only two workers collected in 1977 (deposited at the at the La Plata Museum MLP), and consulted pollination ecologists and bee biologists working intensively in the field in the last 20 years have never seen this species in Buenos Aires (J.P. Torretta, D. Medan, pers. comm.). The species has also been recorded (only 7 specimens in 4 localities) on the Atlantic coast of the provinces of Rio Negro, Chubut and Santa Cruz. Thus, the scarcity of records north from the Rio Colorado in Argentina, suggests that this is the natural northern limit of distribution of the species in Argentina, and the that records in Buenos Aires and Mendoza may have resulted from small and isolated subpopulations in microhabitats suitable for this species (Abrahamovich et al. 2007). Furthermore, subpopulations  in both provinces surveyed in the past are probably extinct. Moreover the few records at the Atlantic coast, and the lack of records in the Patagonia arid steppe, suggest this species is associated with forest and humid habitats.

The southern limit of this species is located in the Navarino Island (a small islet in the Beagle Channel south of Tierra del Fuego Island), in the Chilean XII Region, (queen deposited at  the American Museum of Natural History, AMNH_BEE00147245; Isla Navarino, Chile; Septiembre 1935; J. Bird). It is noteworthy that this location is the global southernmost limit of the Bombus genus.

According to Abrahamovich et al. (2007), in Argentina this species probably also spread south from Santa Cruz, reaching the Argentinean portion of Tierra del Fuego Island (Tierra del Fuego Province), although there is no record in this province. In an intensive field 5 day-survey in January 2012 in the southern part of Tierra del Fuego Province, M.A. Arbetman (pers. obs.) did not find any specimens; neither have the consulted park rangers and scientists working in the region (Arbetman pers. comm.). Thus, so far, the known Argentinian southern limit of its distribution coincides with the tip of the continental territory of Argentina, at the northern coast of the Magellan Strait in Santa Cruz Province (worker deposited at the Museo de La Plata, collected near Río Gallegos city, in 1936). This is also the southeast global limit of distribution of this species. 

The global western limit is in the Pacific Ocean Islands (Chiloé Island) in the X Chilean Region. The species reaches the Pacific coast along all the Chilean regions where it has been recorded.

The reports of this species' presence in Peru by Peat et al. (2005) seem to be the result of a misidentification of the species or the site of collection being incorrectly labelled. Paul Williams (pers. comm.) suggested that queens of B. rubicundus recorded in the High Andes of Peru might pose a misidentification with B. dahlbomii. No additional pinned specimens were found in the MNHL, nor any specimen labelled as collected from Peru (Mr. David Notton pers. comm.). Rasmussen (2003) did not include B. dahlbomii in the key of bumblebee species of Peru either. Therefore we can discard Peru as a country of occurrence of B. dahlbomii and therefore uncertain data from Peru has been excluded for this assessment.

Finally, this species has been reported as mislabelled specimens from Panama by Milliron 1973a:165, and wrongly reported from Santos, Brazil by Handlirsch (1888:236), both quoted by Abrahamovich and Diaz (2002).

Population [top]

Population:

Bombus dahlbomii was reported as abundant in the past, both in Argentina (Abrahamovich and Diaz 2001) and Chile (Montealegre 1927). However, there is a strong consensus, based on independent studies and observations, on a consistent decline of the species' abundance in both countries and extinction of local subpopulations in particular in the north-eastern part of its geographic range, leading to a range loss of >54% of the EOO in the last 10 years (Morales et al. 2013, Schmid-Hempel et al. 2013, Geslin and Morales 2015, see also Distribution section).

In Argentina, there is evidence of a strong decline in the northern portion of its range, where this species has been almost completely replaced by invasive European Bombus terrestris and to a lesser extent B. ruderatus (Arbetman et al. 2012, Morales et al. 2013, Schmid-Hempel et al. 2013, Geslin and Morales 2015). Since the invasion of these introduced bumblebee species, B. dahlbomii has become much harder to find. B. terrestrius was introduced to Chile in 1997, and slowly advanced southward arriving in the range of B. dahlbomii around 2006; B. ruderatus was introduced to South America in the 1980s and arrived in Patagonia around 1994 (Arbetman et al. 2012). In Mendoza, no further record exists after the unique (and Argentinian northernmost) record for that province in the Picheuta River reported by Abrahamovich and Diaz (2001). A recent broad scale survey performed in January-February 2011 (austral summer) along the east slope of the Andes from North Neuquén to South Santa Cruz, failed to find B. dahlbomii in areas where it was reported as abundant until 2004 (e.g. Challhuaco Valley and Llao Llao Forest, in Río Negro Province, Aizen 2001, Morales and Aizen 2002, 2006, Vazquez and Simberloff 2003, Madjifian et al. 2004) and 2006 (e.g. near San Martin de los Andes, in Neuquén Province, Torreta et al. 2006), (Arbetman et al. 2012, Morales et al. 2013). A comparison of the results of that survey with published maps of historical distribution based on museum collections (Abrahamovich and Diaz 2001, see also Distribution), shows that many subpopulations of the northern part of its historic geographic range (Neuquen and Río Negro and to a lesser extent Chubut) have suffered drastic reductions, or even disappeared, being replaced by invasive Bombus terrestris and to a lesser extent by B. ruderatus. In 2011 only the southernmost subpopulations in southern Santa Cruz were still numerous, which coincide with the only locations on which B. terrestris had not arrived yet (see Figure 2 in Morales et al. 2013, see also Schmid-Hempel et al. 2013). More worrisome, an even more recent survey performed in December 2013 shows that these locations have been recently invaded by B. terrestris, which in just three years has become far more abundant than B. dahlbomii (Geslin and Morales 2015).

A two decade long study (1994-2013) at the Challhuaco Valley (NahuelHuapi National Park, Río Negro, Argentina), where B. dahlbomii was the most frequent pollinator species of the lily Alstroemeria aurea in its largest monospecific stand known, reveals that since these records started in 1994 the abundance of this subpopulation declined until it disappeared. Although B. dahlbomii was very abundant and common in this site during the mid-nineties (Aizen 2001 and references therein) the species started to decline after B. ruderatus invasion (Morales 2007, Madjidian et al. 2008); this negative trend was exacerbated with B. terrestris invasion. In fact, no single specimen has been recorded during comprehensive pollinator censuses since 2008, that is, one year after B. terrestris invasion in this area (Arbetman et al. 2012, Morales et al. 2013). Therefore, the collapse of this once very large subpopulation was concurrent with the sequential invasion of the introduced European species Bombus ruderatus (Madjidianet al. 2008) and posterior invasion of Bombus terrestris (Arbetman et al. 2012, Morales et al. 2013).

In Chile, in spite of the lack of an overall quantitative assessment there have been reports of decline throughout most of its historical range, this decline being stronger in the IV (i.e. the global northern limit of the species range), V and Metropolitan Regions of Central Chile (Ruz and Vivallo 2005, Montalva et al. 2011), where J. Montalva (pers. comm.) argues that currently only small and isolated subpopulations persist. Following, we provide an account of the most up-to-date available information region by region (from North to South):

- In the IV Region (Coquimbo), the species was never abundant and always constrained to relictual mountain forest areas (Toro and Chiappa 1997). Recently (Jan. 2013), P. Novoa (pers. com.) photographically documented that this species is still extant in this Region (in the location of Los Vilos) although very scarce.

- In the V Region (Valparaiso), although a recent survey of entomological collections (Smith-Ramirez pers. obs.) revealed that the last specimens of this species were collected in 1974, more recently there have been more reports of B. dahlbomii in the V Region (Montalva pers. comm.). However, all of these partial observations coincide in the declining trend experimented by B. dahlbomii in this region. A study of foraging activities of Apoidea in localities of Central-South (V Region) and South Chile (VIII Region), showed that in 2000 B. dahlbomii was the most abundant species recorded (50%, n=812 bees), whereas the invasive B. terrestris accounted for only 10% (n=170 bees) of the apoidea (Ruz and Herrera 2001); this invasive species was present in the V Region, but not in the VIII Region. In agreement with that study, and according to collecting records, in 2002 B. dahlbomii was abundant in sites like Laguna Verde, La Ligua, Palmar El Salto and Quebrada Alvarado. However, nowadays it is no longer found in most of these areas, where there are are now plenty of B. terrestris (Montalva pers. comm.). Notable exceptions are some recent reports of B. dahlbomii from relictual forests in Quebrada Alvarado, in La Campana National Park (J. Montalva pers. comm.), and El Salto, where it is still present, though declining. For instance, in 2009-2010 B. dahlbomii was already scarce in the Botanical Garden of El Salto, whereas B. terrestris was already abundant. Overall, in 2010 the relative abundance of this species compared to that reported by Ruz and Herrera ten years ago (2001) had strongly dropped in the region; E. Chiappa (pers. comm.) collected only one B. dahlbomii out 1,115 bees (0.09%) in Quebrada La Horquilla (QuebradaVerde) and only one out of 249 bees (0.4%) in Quebrada El Sapo (El Salto). No B. terrestris were collected in these localities during that survey, which is also intriguing given that according to L. Ruz (pers. comm.) in 2005-2006 B. terrestris was scarce, but already present in a nearby site (Laguna Verde) .More recently (2013) various specimens have been observed in the localities of Casablanca ( S. Rodriguez pers. comm.) and Curacaví (J. Solervicens pers. comm.). This species has not been observed in Villa Alemana (Carmen Tobar-PUCV), Gabriel Dazarola (PUCV).

- In the Metropolitan region B. dahlbomii was reported as abundant in the Cerro San Cristobal (Santiago de Chile City) during the first quarter of the XX century (Ruiz 1923), but the last collections from the Metropolitan Regions were from 1996 (Smith-Ramirez pers. comm.). Currently the species has been reported for some cordilleran sectors like Yerba Loca, Valle Nevado and Farellones (J. Montalvapers. comm.), but it is no longer observed in the valley of the Metropolitan Region, which is dominated by B. terrestris (Montalva 2010). Some specimens has been observed recently (2013) by S. Rodríguez (pers. comm.).

- In the VI Region, similarly to the Metropolitan Region, B. dahlbomii seems to be locally extinct in the central Valley, but still present in the Cordillera. 

- In the VII Region, B. dahlbomii is very scarce, and only can be observed in parks of cordilleran valleys, and is absent from urban areas where B. terrestris is very abundant (C. Avendaño pers. comm.)

- In the VIII Region, according to a study carried out in Cobquecura, near the Pacific coast in 2000 Bombus dahlbomii was the most abundant apoidea (>50%) and only bumblebee visiting Eryngium paniculatum (Ruz and Herrera 2001), while in 2005 the species was absent in the same area and the main pollinators of E. paniculatum were B. terrestris and B. ruderatus (L. Ruzpers. comm.). However, in 2013 (C. Tobar pers. comm.) did not observe any B. dahlbomii, or B. ruderatus, but only B. terrestris. Thus, B. dahlbomii seems to have been progressively been replaced by B. ruderatus, and later by B. terrestris, as has been reported for Challhuaco Valley, in Argentina (Morales et al. 2013).

- In the IX Region, B. dahlbomii was recorded only twice in a recent survey on blueberry orchards, being very scarce in agricultural areas, and slightly more abundant in cordilleran areas with native forests. The whole area is largely dominated by B. terrestris and B. ruderatus (L. Vieli pers. comm.). In addition, in the Nahuelbuta National Park, where just seven years ago B. dahlbomii was abundant, L. Packer (pers. comm.) could not see any individual, in contrast seeing only B. terrestris.

- In the X Region, in Chiloé Island a long term survey since 2000 revealed that until 2008 this species was abundant, and declined after that, coinciding with the arrival on this island of the invasive B. terrestris (Smith-Ramirez, 2014).

- In the XI Region, in Coihaique B. dahlbomii was very abundant in the past, and approximately five years ago started to decline. Now B. terrestris is very abundant (Piere Barattini pers. comm.).

- Finally, in the XII Region, B. dahlbomii is still very abundant near Punta Arenas (Leah Dudley pers. comm., Tieve et al. accepted), and there have been recent observations of the species in Parque Karukinka, Tierra del Fuego (R. Muza pers. comm.). According to J. Montalva (pers. obs.) this is the only region of Chile where B. terrestris has not arrived yet, which coincides with the southward increasing abundance of B. dahlbomii and decreasing abundance of B. terrestris reported by Morales et al. (2013) in Argentina.

A recent citizen science campaign launched by “Salvemos nuestro Abejorro” Inititative (http://salvemosnuestroabejorro.wordpress.com) to encourage volunteers to submit photographic records of this species has been useful in proving that this species is still extant in most of its range, in spite of reduction of abundance.

To summarize, although scattered and fragmented, many independent records and observations from Argentina and Chile point to a substantial drop of B. dahlbomii abundance and range and even its complete disappearance in some sites where it was formerly common.

Habitat and Ecology [top]

Habitat and Ecology:

This species occupies the southernwest most portion of continental South America. As a consequence, it is the southernmost bumblebee species in the world. This is a typical species of the Broadleaf and Mixed forest biome (according to Olson 2001 classification of global biomes), and in the near past it was very common in the following terrestrial ecoregions: Valdivian Temperate Forests, Magellanic Subpolar Forests (both ecoregions characterized by broadleaf Nothofagus spp. forest types), more or less common in the ecoregions of Chilean Matorral (associated to remnant and relictual hygrophyllous forests) and less common in particular suitable habitats in the Southern Andes Steppe and Patagonia Steppe. It has also been frequently recorded in open vegetation communities of lake margins (C. Morales pers. obs.) and mountain meadows. Although there have been a few records in the ecoregions of Argentine Monte, Argentine Spinal and humid Pampas, it is probably extirpated from all these ecoregions. 

This species nests in cavities in the soil, below trunks, sphagnum, soil and roots, and nests are small compared with those of other Bombus species (Claude-Joseph 1926, Montealegre 1927, Ruiz 1939, Montalva et al. 2011). This species was commonly observed foraging in gardens of small towns, although nests have not been reported in such urban areas. Regarding foraging ecology, B. dahlbomii is a long tongued bumblebee, that visits flowers of a broad spectrum of colours, sizes and shapes of a high diversity of plant families, including Fabaceae, Asteraceae, Myrtaceae, Lamiaceae and Rosaceae among other common families (Montalva et al. 2011, Abrahamovich et al. 2001, Morales unpublished). Overall, this species has been recorded in more than 90 native and introduced plant species of >40 families (Abrahamovich et al. 2001, Morales and Aizen 2010, Montalva et al. 2011).

Bombus dahlbomii has been shown to play a key role in the structure and function of plant-pollinator networks of many communities (Vazquez and Simberloff 2003, Morales and Aizen 2006, Aizen et al. 2008, Ramos-Jiliberto et al. 2009). For instance, model simulations from a recent analysis of plant-pollinator networks revealed the importance of hymenopterans as pivotal groups that maintain the core structure of the pollination network and guarantee overall species persistence in the temperate forests of Chiloé Island. Among them, Bombus dahlbomii exerted a disproportionately large influence on the preservation of network structure. Thus, we foresee its extinction would seriously harm the reproductive success of plants in this temperate rain forest, and therefore recommend that B. dahlbomii should be considered as focal species for conservation programs given current threats from selective logging and habitat loss (Ramos-Jiliberto et al. 2009). In addition, B. dahlbomii is a key pollinator of many endemic plants like the lily Alstroemeria aurea (Aizen 2001).

With respect to relevant aspects of bee biology and genetics, in colonial bees only queens and drones are reproductive. In addition, Bombus dahlbomii, as with the rest of the social bees, can show a reduced heterozygosity due to the low effective population size. However, to estimate the effective population size in haplo-diploids is a complex task as a result of atypical and unknown sex ratio effects. No work has been done on this topic for this species, but it is safe to assume that effective population sizes are smaller and more vulnerable in most of the haplo-diploids than for diplo-diploid organisms with similar sex ratios and apparent population sizes.

Use and Trade [top]

Use and Trade:

This species has been experimentally reared in Chile for a couple of decades (Estay et al. 2001, Olavarria Morales 2003), after which the rearing methodlogy has been recently patented by the Ing. Patricia Estay, head of the Entomology Laboratory of  the National Institute of Agronomic and Food research of Chile (INIA, La Platina), (http://www.inia.cl/blog/2014/09/01/inapi-otorgo-patente-a-invencion-inia-para-domesticacion-y-crianza-de-abejorros-para-polinizacion/). However it is uncertain whether hives of this species are being commercially produced or subject to trade.

Threats [top]

Major Threat(s):

The most important and documented threat to the species at present is the invasion of European bumblebee species introduced for crop pollination, namely B. terrestris and to a lesser extent B. ruderatus (Morales 2007, Morales et al. in press, Stout and Morales 2009). The driver of the threat is the global trade of non native bumblebee species for pollination of agricultural field and greenhouse crops. The industry allows for the introduction of massively reared bumblebee colonies outside their native range and the subsequent risk of escape of mated queens and establishment of founder populations.

At sites where B. dahlbomii was formerly abundant and now is no longer observed or is very scarce, B. terrestris is extremely abundant (Morales et al. 2013, Schmid-Hempel et al. 2013, Geslin and Morales 2015,  J. Montalva, Luisa Ruz pers. obs., C. Avendaño, L. Vieli, Dr. Packer, pers. comm.). Hypothesized mechanisms underlying this observed ecological replacement include pathogen transmission (Arbetman et al. 2013, Schmid-Hempel et al. 2015), although competition for resources cannot be discarded (Morales et al. 2013). 

There is evidence that the highly lethal bumblebee pathogen Apicystis bombi, (detected in B. terrestris populations of southern Argentina but not in other native bumblebees of other regions of Argentina (Plischck and Lange 2009)) shares the same haplotypes with Apicystis of bumblebees collected in Europe (Maharranov et al. 2013). This pathogen seems to have been co-introduced with invasive B. terrestris, from which it might have jumped to B. dahlbomii, which was free of this parasite before B. terrestris invasion (Arbetman et al. 2013). In a similar way, other diseases and parasites may have also been transmitted by B. ruderatus when this species invaded the region. 

Bombus terrestris has larger colonies, emerges earlier from hibernation, has a longer season of activity than B. dahlbomii (Morales et al. 2013). Thus competitive displacement seems plausible, although it has not been experimentally demonstrated, partly because of the rapid collapse of B. dahlbomii subpopulations after B. terrestris invasion.

Bombus terrestris distribution overlaps with almost all the range of B. dahlbomii, with the exception of the XII Region (Magallanes) in Chile, which might host the only subpopulations of B. dahlbomii still free from the invasive B. terrestris (Montalva pers. obs., Tieve et al. accepted). Furthermore, in a great portion of the historic range of B. dahlbomii, B. terrestris is by far more abundant than the native species (Morales et al. 2013). Given the surprisingly high rate of spread of B. terrestris in southern South America, and the fact that this species has already reached almost the whole B. dahlbomii distribution range, this threat can certainly affect the global population.

Other focal threats that can affect local subpopulations are the increasing habitat loss and urbanization rate (in particular in Central Chile, Arroyo et al. 1999). Habitat fragmentation and disturbance seems to indirectly affect the species by favouring the spread of Bombus ruderatus (Aizen and Feinsinger 2002), and Bombus terrestris (Morales unpublished). Finally, global warming may negatively affect and probably accelerate the observed southward retraction observed at the northern part its range.

Conservation Actions [top]

Conservation Actions:

Many of the remaining Argentinian subpopulations as well as many of the Chilean subpopulations are located in National Parks and other Natural and Wildlife Reserves. However, if invasion of introduced bumblebees are the main drivers of its decline, solely protecting habitat will probably not be effective enough to revert this declining trend, because these invasive species also thrive in these habitats. 

In addition there have been a lot of outreach activities (radio interviews, talks, etc.) and publications (Morales 2006a,b, 2007, 2009; Arbetman and Morales 2014) aimed to enhance the public awareness on this species.

In Chile a targeted Campaign called "Salvenos Nuestro Abejorro" (Save our bumblebee) has been launched by Jose Montalva: https://salvemosnuestroabejorro.wordpress.com/. In addition, a proposal to classify and protect this species under the National Red Listing Scheme in Chile has been recently submitted by Jose Montalva as Endangered (Montalva et al. unpublished report).

In Argentina an educational brochure "El mangangá y la polinización de nuestros bosques" has been designed by Carolina L. Morales and published with the support of the Cannon National Parks Science Scholar Program, the National Parks Administration of Argentina and the Universidad Nacional del Comahue.

There are no other conservation actions currently in place apart from current research on population numbers and trends, threats and uses. Some proposed realistic actions needed to mitigate major threats to the taxon are to stop the importation of non-native bumblebee species, control and eradicate the currently established populations of Bombus terrestris and B. ruderatus in areas where subpopulations of B. dahlbomii are still healthy (manual removal of colonies/mated queens), identify subpopulations of B. dahlbomii which are immune or resistant to Apicystis bombi that can be used for captive breeding and reintroduction programs, study rearing techniques specific to the native species, genetic analysis of populations where still abundant, to inform management programs. Another possible action to reduce populations of invasive Bombus spp. might be to release sterilized drones or males to reduce the fecundity of the species as has been successful in other taxa.
