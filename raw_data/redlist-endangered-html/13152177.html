Assessment Information [top]

Red List Category & Criteria: Endangered B1ac(iv)+2ac(iv) ver 3.1
Year Published: 2013
Date Assessed: 2012-09-17
Assessor(s): Vasconcelos, R.
Reviewer(s): Bowles, P.
Justification:
Listed as Endangered on the basis that this gecko has an extent of occurrence, and maximum area of occupancy, of 10 km2, it is known from two locations defined by threats from exploitation of its prey species, and extreme fluctuations in the number of mature individuals has been inferred from extreme fluctuations in populations of prey and commensal species.

Geographic Range [top]

Range Description:This predominantly nocturnal gecko is restricted to Branco and Raso, two small islets in the Cape Verde Islands (Vasconcelos et al. 2012) with a combined land area of 10 km2. On Raso, the larger of the two islands, it is mostly confined to the southern coast and central plain (Andreone 2000, Schleich et al. 1983). Subfossil evidence from owl pellets and other remains indicates that the species was once more widespread, but that it disappeared from the diets of predators on São Vicente and Santa Luzia following human settlement and the introduction of mice (Carranza et al. 2000, Mateo et al. 2009).

Population [top]

Population:This species has been reported as being locally common to abundant in both historical (1980s) and more recent literature (Schleich, 1980, 1982; Mateo et al. 1997; Vasconcelos et al. 2010). Schleich (1980) estimated the total population of Tarentola gigas brancoensis, which is confined to the smaller of the two islets, as containing "about two hundred animals", and that this subspecies occurs in "higher densities (some hundreds)" in the islet's south (Schleich 1982). The population is expected to fluctuate strongly, probably by at least an order of magnitude, due to fluctuating pressures on the populations of its prey species (Donald et al. 2003, R. Vasconcelos pers. comm. 2012).
Current Population Trend:Stable

Habitat and Ecology [top]

Habitat and Ecology:This species is commonly found in cliff-holes and burrows used by seabirds, typically beneath sandstone blocks (Hazevoet 1995); it avoids rough and fissured rock, and wind-exposed slopes (Schleich 1980). It feeds on the broken eggs and possibly young of nesting birds, being the major (and perhaps only) natural predator of eggs of the Raso Lark Alauda razae, a bird now restricted to Raso islet (Donald et al. 2003). On Branco, where the Raso Lark no longer occurs, it presumably relies primarily on colonies of the Cape Verde Shearwater (Calonectris edwardsii).
Systems:Terrestrial

Use and Trade [top]

Use and Trade: This is a large and charismatic gecko, and pet trade websites in the US and Japan offer animals for sale (R. Vasconcelos pers. obs.). It is unknown where these animals are sourced or whether captive breeding is taking place, but it is presumed that wild-caught animals form part of this trade.

Threats [top]

Major Threat(s): Infrastructure development, improved access to refrigeration technology, and the depletion of fish stocks following an increase in international fishing in Cape Verde's waters have all prompted local fisherman to extend their fishing expeditions on the uninhabited Raso islet, as it takes longer to catch profitable volumes of fish and the catch can be stored for longer periods. The most important consequence of increased human pressure on the islet for T. gigas is an increase in pressure on shearwater populations; particularly when fishing is poor, fishermen turn to seabird populations for sustenance and income, preferentially targeting the young and eggs on which the gecko depends (Donald et al. 2003). An estimated 5,000 nestlings are harvested annually (Nunes and Hazeveot 2001), however, this number fluctuates heavily. In 2008, 12,700 birds were taken from Raso (Agência Lusa 2008). This lizard's only other prey species, the Raso Lark, has a population which is subject to extreme, climate-driven fluctuations on the drought-prone islet, and which is presently listed as Critically Endangered (BirdLife International 2012). Research access to Branco is difficult, but it is likely that pressures on the shearwater colony of the smaller islet are similar to those affecting the population on Raso. The Raso Lark is extinct on Branco.

ONG Biosfera I report a common practice of abandoning unwanted kittens on Raso, where they are expected to die due to the harsh conditions. No reproducing cats are known to exist on Raso, but the increased human presence on the islet increases the likelihood that this invasive species will become established here (Donald et al. 2003). The introduction of rodents to these islands also poses a potential risk to both the gecko and its prey species.

Conservation Actions [top]

Conservation Actions: This species was listed as Endangered in the First Red List of Cape Verde, which considered this species in need of urgent protection (Schleich 1996). Despite this recommendation, and the subsequent formal protection of the islets, there is poor government enforcement of permit requirements or effective controls on access, other than scientific collection, and legislation is required to improve protection of Raso and Branco. Control of invasive species is required in areas where these have or may become established. All native reptiles in the Cape Verde archipelago are endemic to these islands, although this is not widely-recognized among the public. Education is required to raise awareness of these species and their conservation requirements. This species should be included in CITES legislation to monitor and, if necessary, control international trade in this endangered gecko. Geckos are feared due to traditional beliefs, and there may be occasional persecution; education is therefore needed to raise awareness that these animals are inoffensive to humans. Existing restrictions (enshrined in Cape Verdean Regulatory Decree no. 7/2002) on killing animals or their eggs, disturbance to or destruction of their habitats and disturbance to the animals themselves should be implemented. Research is urgently required to obtain data on this gecko's natural history, the impacts of apparent threats to this lizard and its prey species, and its population status. Research is recommended to investigate the possibility of its reintroduction on Santa Luzia.
